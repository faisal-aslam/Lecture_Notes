Some people assume that quantum computers do not exist and that we only have theory. In fact, there is now a large variety of different kinds of quantum computers. Some use photons, while others use atomic particles such as electrons or trapped ions. The primary reason these computers are not yet breaking cryptographic systems based on large integers, or solving large-scale discrete logarithm problems on elliptic curves, is the presence of quantum errors. Due to these errors, a large number of qubits must be devoted to detecting and correcting errors rather than performing useful computations.

In summary, the key reason quantum computers have not yet fully achieved their potential is the presence of quantum errors. Therefore, researchers are currently investing tremendous effort into developing quantum hardware and quantum gates that produce fewer errors, as well as quantum error-correction techniques that can protect quantum information with the smallest possible overhead.
