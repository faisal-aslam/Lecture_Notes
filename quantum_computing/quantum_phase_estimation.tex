\section{Motivation: Why Eigenvalues Matter in Quantum Computing}
\label{sec:motivation}

\subsection{The Central Problem}

Many of the most important problems in science and engineering reduce to
\emph{eigenvalue problems}.  In chemistry, the ground-state energy of a
molecule is the smallest eigenvalue of its Hamiltonian.  In number theory,
the period of a function (the key to factoring large integers) is related to
the eigenvalues of a unitary operator.  In linear systems, stability is
governed by eigenvalues.

Classically, finding eigenvalues of an $N \times N$ matrix costs at least
$\bigO(N^2)$ time and often much more.  For quantum systems, $N$ grows
\emph{exponentially} in the number of particles — making the classical
approach completely infeasible even for molecules with a few dozen electrons.

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{The key insight.}\ Quantum Phase Estimation (\QPE) solves a specific
  version of the eigenvalue problem: given a unitary operator $U$ and
  (approximately) one of its eigenstates $\Ket{\psi}$, estimate the
  corresponding eigenvalue $e^{2\pi \ii \varphi}$ to $n$ bits of precision
  using only $\bigO(n)$ queries to a controlled-$U$ oracle.  This is
  exponentially faster than any known classical algorithm for the same task.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{The Eigenvalue of a Unitary Operator}

Recall from linear algebra:

\begin{definition}{Unitary Operator and Its Eigenvalues}{}
  An operator $U$ on $\C^N$ is \textbf{unitary} if
  $U^\dagger U = U U^\dagger = \Id$, where $U^\dagger$ denotes the conjugate
  transpose of $U$ and $\Id$ is the $N\times N$ identity operator.
  A non-zero vector $\Ket{\psi} \in \C^N$ is an \textbf{eigenstate} of $U$
  with \textbf{eigenvalue} $\lambda \in \C$ if
  \[
    U\Ket{\psi} = \lambda \Ket{\psi}.
  \]
  Because $U$ is unitary, every eigenvalue satisfies $|\lambda| = 1$
  (proved below), so we may write
  \[
    \lambda = e^{2\pi \ii \varphi}, \quad \varphi \in [0,1).
  \]
  The real number $\varphi$ is called the \textbf{(relative) phase} of the eigenvalue.
  It is defined modulo $1$, i.e.\ $\varphi$ and $\varphi + 1$ encode the same eigenvalue.
\end{definition}

\medskip

\textbf{Why $|\lambda|=1$?}\ If $U\Ket{\psi}=\lambda\Ket{\psi}$, then
applying the bra from the left and using unitarity:
\[
  \Bra{\psi}U^\dagger U\Ket{\psi} = \Bra{\psi}\Ket{\psi} = 1,
\]
but also
\[
  \Bra{\psi}U^\dagger U\Ket{\psi}
  = (U\Ket{\psi})^\dagger(U\Ket{\psi})
  = (\lambda\Ket{\psi})^\dagger(\lambda\Ket{\psi})
  = \lambda^*\lambda\,\Braket{\psi|\psi}
  = |\lambda|^2.
\]
Hence $|\lambda|^2 = 1$, so $|\lambda| = 1$.  Every eigenvalue of every
unitary operator lies on the complex unit circle, fully characterised by
the single real number $\varphi \in [0,1)$.

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{Rephrasing the goal.}\ \QPE{} finds $\varphi$ given $U$ and
  $\Ket{\psi}$.  Every quantum algorithm that claims an exponential speedup
  via \QPE{} ultimately reduces its hard problem to: \emph{``find this phase.''}
  The rest of these notes explain, from first principles, exactly how.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Running Example Setup}
\label{subsec:running}

Throughout this lecture we use the following concrete example, small enough
to trace entirely by hand yet rich enough to illustrate every subtlety.

\begin{tcolorbox}[example, title={Running Example: the $T$ Gate}]
  The single-qubit \textbf{$T$ gate} (also called the $\pi/8$ gate) is:
  \[
    T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{\ii\pi/4} \end{pmatrix}.
  \]
  \textbf{Eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $T$:}
  Since $T$ is diagonal, its eigenvalues are its diagonal entries:
  $\lambda_0 = 1$ (eigenvector $\Ket{0}$) and
  $\lambda_1 = e^{\ii\pi/4}$ (eigenvector $\Ket{1}$).

  \medskip
  \textbf{Identifying the phase $\varphi$:}\ Writing $\lambda_1 = e^{2\pi \ii \varphi}$:
  \[
    e^{2\pi \ii \varphi} = e^{\ii\pi/4}
    \;\Longrightarrow\;
    2\pi\varphi = \frac{\pi}{4}
    \;\Longrightarrow\;
    \varphi = \frac{1}{8}.
  \]
  \textbf{In binary:}\ $\varphi = 1/8 = 0.001_2$ (since $\frac{0}{2}+\frac{0}{4}+\frac{1}{8}=\frac{1}{8}$).

  \medskip
  \textbf{QPE setup:}\ We run \QPE{} with $U = T$, eigenstate $\Ket{\psi}=\Ket{1}$,
  and $n = 3$ ancilla qubits.  With $n=3$, we can represent $2^3=8$ distinct
  values $\{0, \tfrac{1}{8}, \tfrac{2}{8}, \ldots, \tfrac{7}{8}\}$.  Since
  $\varphi=\tfrac{1}{8}$ is exactly representable, we expect to measure
  the binary string $001$ (encoding the integer $f=1$, giving
  $\tilde\varphi = 1/8$) \emph{with probability 1}.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Prerequisites and Notation}
\label{sec:prereqs}

Even though students in this course know the basics, it is worth fixing
notation precisely — small sign or convention errors accumulate and produce
incorrect final results.

\subsection{The Computational Basis and Dirac Notation}

An $n$-qubit \textbf{computational basis state} is written $\Ket{j}$ where
$j \in \{0,1,\ldots,2^n-1\}$ is the decimal value of the corresponding
binary string.  The binary string is ordered from \emph{most significant
bit (MSB) on the left} to \emph{least significant bit (LSB) on the right}:
\[
  \Ket{j} = \Ket{j_{n-1}\, j_{n-2}\, \cdots\, j_1\, j_0},
  \quad j = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} j_k\, 2^k, \quad j_k \in \{0,1\}.
\]
For example, with $n=3$: $\Ket{6} = \Ket{110}$ since
$1\cdot4 + 1\cdot2 + 0\cdot1 = 6$.

\subsection{Binary Fractions}
\label{subsec:binaryfrac}

A crucial notational convenience: the \textbf{binary fraction}
\[
  0.b_1 b_2 \cdots b_n \;:=\; \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{b_k}{2^k}
    = \frac{b_1}{2} + \frac{b_2}{4} + \cdots + \frac{b_n}{2^n}, \quad b_k\in\{0,1\}.
\]
Examples: $0.1_2 = 1/2$;\ \ $0.01_2 = 1/4$;\ \ $0.101_2 = 1/2 + 1/8 = 5/8$.

The phase $\varphi$ will often be expressed as such a fraction.  Note the
relationship to the integer encoding: if the phase register contains the
integer $j = j_{n-1}\cdots j_0$ in binary, then
\[
  \frac{j}{2^n} = 0.j_{n-1}\,j_{n-2}\cdots j_0.
\]

\subsection{The Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT)}
\label{subsec:qft}

\QPE{} uses the QFT as a subroutine.  We review it carefully here.

\begin{definition}{Quantum Fourier Transform}{}
  The $n$-qubit \textbf{Quantum Fourier Transform} (\QFT) is the unitary
  operator defined by its action on computational basis states:
  \[
    \QFT\Ket{j} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}
    \sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii jk/2^n}\Ket{k}, \qquad j \in \{0,\ldots,2^n-1\}.
  \]
  The \textbf{inverse QFT} (\IQFT) satisfies $\IQFT = \QFT^\dagger$ and acts as:
  \[
    \IQFT\Ket{j} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}
    \sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} e^{-2\pi \ii jk/2^n}\Ket{k}.
  \]
  In the \textbf{product form} (essential for circuit implementation), the
  QFT of a computational basis state $\Ket{j_1 j_2 \cdots j_n}$
  (where $j_1$ is the MSB and $j_n$ is the LSB) is:
  \[
    \QFT\Ket{j_1 j_2 \cdots j_n}
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}
      \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii\, 0.j_n}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
      \otimes
      \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii\, 0.j_{n-1}j_n}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
      \otimes \cdots \otimes
      \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii\, 0.j_1 j_2 \cdots j_n}\Ket{1}\Bigr).
  \]
\end{definition}

\medskip

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{Why the product form matters.}\ The product form shows that
  the output qubits of the QFT carry \emph{different amounts} of phase
  information about the binary fraction $0.j_1 j_2 \cdots j_n$.
  Specifically, the $k$-th factor in the tensor product is in the state
  $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\,0.j_{n-k+1}\cdots j_n}\Ket{1})$,
  encoding the last $k$ bits of the input.
  At one extreme, the \emph{last} output qubit (the $n$-th factor) encodes
  only $j_n$ — a single bit of $\varphi$.
  At the other extreme, the \emph{first} output qubit (the 1st factor)
  encodes all $n$ bits via the full binary fraction $0.j_1 j_2\cdots j_n$.
  This hierarchical structure — from coarsest resolution at the first qubit
  to finest resolution at the last — is what makes the QFT efficient to
  implement in $\bigO(n^2)$ gates (each qubit only needs controlled-phase
  interactions with the qubits that follow it in the product), and is also
  what allows \QPE{} to decode the phase: Stage~2 of \QPE{} prepares a
  state in \emph{exactly} this product form, and the \IQFT{} then reads
  it out as a computational basis state.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Controlled-Unitary Gates}

\begin{definition}{Controlled-$U$ Gate}{}
  Let $U$ be a unitary acting on $m$ qubits.  The \textbf{controlled-$U$}
  gate $\cU$ acts on one control qubit and $m$ target qubits as:
  \[
    \cU \;\Ket{c}\Ket{x} =
    \begin{cases}
      \Ket{0}\Ket{x}     & \text{if } c=0, \\
      \Ket{1} U\Ket{x}   & \text{if } c=1.
    \end{cases}
  \]
  In operator form:
  $\cU = \Ket{0}\!\Bra{0}\otimes \Id_m + \Ket{1}\!\Bra{1}\otimes U$.
  Here $\Id_m$ is the identity on the $m$-qubit target space.
\end{definition}

\medskip

\subsubsection{Phase Kickback}

Now observe the crucial phenomenon that underlies all of \QPE{}.
Let the control qubit be in the equal superposition
$\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\Ket{0}+\Ket{1})$ and let the target be in the
eigenstate $\Ket{\psi}$ with $U\Ket{\psi}=e^{2\pi \ii \varphi}\Ket{\psi}$:
\begin{align}
  \cU \;\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\otimes\Ket{\psi}
  &= \frac{\Ket{0}\!\otimes\!\Id\Ket{\psi}+\Ket{1}\!\otimes\! U\Ket{\psi}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \notag \\
  &= \frac{\Ket{0}\Ket{\psi}+e^{2\pi \ii \varphi}\Ket{1}\Ket{\psi}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \notag \\
  &= \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii \varphi}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\otimes\Ket{\psi}.
  \label{eq:phase_kickback}
\end{align}

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{Phase Kickback.}\ Equation~\eqref{eq:phase_kickback} is the single
  most important mechanism in \QPE.  The phase $e^{2\pi \ii \varphi}$
  \emph{migrates from the target to the control qubit}, while the target
  $\Ket{\psi}$ is returned \emph{completely unchanged}.

  This is why:
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item We use $\Ket{\psi}$ as an eigenstate (not a general state):
      kickback only produces a clean phase factor when the target is an
      eigenstate, because $U\Ket{\psi} = e^{2\pi\ii\varphi}\Ket{\psi}$
      leaves $\Ket{\psi}$ undisturbed.
    \item The eigenstate register remains reusable throughout all
      controlled-$U^{2^k}$ applications.
    \item Information about $\varphi$ is deposited into the \emph{phase
      register} (control qubits), where it can be extracted by the \IQFT.
  \end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{The QPE Circuit: Structure and Deep Intuition}
\label{sec:circuit}

\subsection{Circuit Overview}

\QPE{} uses two registers:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Phase register} (ancilla): $n$ qubits, initialised to
        $\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}$.  After the algorithm, measuring this register
        yields an $n$-bit binary string encoding $\tilde\varphi \approx \varphi$.
  \item \textbf{Eigenstate register}: $m$ qubits holding $\Ket{\psi}$,
        the eigenstate of $U$.  This register is untouched by the measurement.
\end{itemize}

The circuit has three stages:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Stage \arabic*.}]
  \item \textbf{Superposition:} Apply $H^{\otimes n}$ to the phase register.
        \emph{Why:} to create a uniform superposition that will allow all
        $n$ phase-kickback operations to work simultaneously.
  \item \textbf{Controlled rotations:} Apply $c\text{-}U^{2^k}$ for
        $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$.  \emph{Why:} to encode the phase $\varphi$
        into the relative amplitudes of the phase register, in exactly the
        form produced by the QFT.
  \item \textbf{Inverse QFT:} Apply $\IQFT$ to the phase register, then
        measure.  \emph{Why:} to convert the QFT-encoded state back into a
        computational basis state that directly reads out $\varphi$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Circuit Diagram}

Below, qubit $q_{n-1}$ is the MSB of the phase register (top wire) and $q_0$
is the LSB (bottom ancilla wire).  The eigenstate register occupies the
bottom $m$ wires.

\[
\begin{quantikz}[row sep=0.40cm, column sep=0.55cm]
  \lstick{$\ket{0}$\;($q_{n-1}$)} & \gate{H} & \ctrl{4} & \qw      & \qw      & \cdots & \qw      & \qw & \gate[4]{\IQFT} & \meter{} \\
  \lstick{$\ket{0}$\;($q_{n-2}$)} & \gate{H} & \qw      & \ctrl{3} & \qw      & \cdots & \qw      & \qw &                 & \meter{} \\
  \lstick{\vdots}                  &          &          &          & \ddots   &        &          &     &                 &          \\
  \lstick{$\ket{0}$\;($q_0$)}     & \gate{H} & \qw      & \qw      & \qw      & \cdots & \ctrl{1} & \qw &                 & \meter{} \\
  \lstick{$\ket{\psi}$}            & \qwbundle{m} & \gate{U^{2^{n-1}}} & \gate{U^{2^{n-2}}} & \gate{U^{2^{n-3}}} & \cdots & \gate{U^{2^{0}}} & \qw & \qw & \qw \\
\end{quantikz}
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Reading the diagram:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The top ancilla qubit ($q_{n-1}$, MSB) controls $U^{2^{n-1}}$, the
        \emph{highest} power.
  \item The bottom ancilla qubit ($q_0$, LSB) controls $U^{2^0}=U$, the
        \emph{lowest} power.
  \item After all controlled gates, the $n$ ancilla qubits undergo the
        \IQFT{}, and each is then measured in the computational basis.
  \item The eigenstate register $\Ket{\psi}$ exits unchanged.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Stage 1 — Creating Superposition: Why Hadamard?}
\label{subsec:stage1}

After applying $H^{\otimes n}$ to $\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}$, the phase register
becomes
\[
  H^{\otimes n}\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}
  = \bigotimes_{k=0}^{n-1} H\Ket{0}
  = \bigotimes_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}} \sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} \Ket{j}.
\]
The last equality follows because expanding $n$ factors of
$(\Ket{0}+\Ket{1})$ produces all $2^n$ binary strings, each with
coefficient $1/\sqrt{2^n}$.

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{Why superposition is essential.}\ If the phase register
  started in $\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}$ (no Hadamard), the controlled-$U^{2^k}$
  gates would do nothing: the control qubit is $\Ket{0}$, so $U$ is never
  applied, and no phase information can be extracted.

  The Hadamard puts each control qubit into the superposition
  $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\Ket{0}+\Ket{1})$.  Now the $\Ket{1}$ branch of
  each qubit \emph{activates} the controlled gate, while the $\Ket{0}$
  branch does not.  Phase kickback then places the phase $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi 2^k}$
  as a relative phase on the $\Ket{1}$ branch.  This is the only way to
  encode continuous phase information into a discrete quantum state.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Stage 2 --- Controlled-\texorpdfstring{$U^{2^k}$}{U^{}2^{}k} Gates: Why These Powers?}
\label{subsec:stage2}

Let qubit $k$ (the $k$-th ancilla from the LSB, $k=0,1,\ldots,n-1$) control
the gate $U^{2^k}$.  Before Stage 2, the full system state is
\[
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1}\Ket{j}\otimes\Ket{\psi}.
\]
By phase kickback (Eq.~\eqref{eq:phase_kickback}), applying controlled-$U^{2^k}$
transforms qubit $k$ as follows (since $U^{2^k}\Ket{\psi} = e^{2\pi\ii\varphi\cdot 2^k}\Ket{\psi}$):
\[
  \frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii \varphi \cdot 2^k}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}},
\]
while $\Ket{\psi}$ remains unchanged.  After all $n$ controlled gates, the
phase register is in the product state:
\[
  \bigotimes_{k=0}^{n-1}\frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii \varphi 2^k}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}.
\]
Expanding this tensor product and using $j = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} 2^k j_k$:
\begin{align}
  \Ket{\Psi_2}
  &= \bigotimes_{k=0}^{n-1}\frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii \varphi 2^k}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \otimes\Ket{\psi}
  \notag \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}} \sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1}
      \prod_{k=0}^{n-1} \left(e^{2\pi \ii \varphi 2^k}\right)^{j_k} \Ket{j}
  \otimes\Ket{\psi}
  \notag \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}} \sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1}
      e^{2\pi \ii \varphi \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} 2^k j_k} \Ket{j}
  \otimes\Ket{\psi}
  \notag \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}} \sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1}
      e^{2\pi \ii \varphi j}\Ket{j}
  \otimes\Ket{\psi}.
  \label{eq:after_controlled}
\end{align}

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{Why powers of 2?}\ The crucial step is the last line of the
  derivation above: the exponent simplifies to $\varphi j$ only because
  $j = \sum_k 2^k j_k$ is the binary expansion of $j$.  By choosing the
  $k$-th gate to apply $U^{2^k}$, we ensure that the accumulated phase for
  basis state $\Ket{j}$ is exactly $e^{2\pi \ii \varphi j}$ — precisely
  the form required by the QFT.  Any other choice of exponents would break
  this identity and the \IQFT{} would not produce a sharp peak.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Stage 3 — Inverse QFT: Why It Decodes the Phase}
\label{subsec:stage3}

After Stage 2, the phase register holds (ignoring $\Ket{\psi}$):
\[
  \Ket{\Phi} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii \varphi j}\Ket{j}.
\]
Now compare this with the definition of the QFT:
\[
  \QFT\Ket{f} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii fj/2^n}\Ket{j}.
\]
Setting $f = \varphi \cdot 2^n$, we see that $\Ket{\Phi} = \QFT\Ket{f}$.
Therefore:
\[
  \IQFT\Ket{\Phi} = \IQFT\bigl(\QFT\Ket{f}\bigr) = \Ket{f}.
\]

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{The \IQFT{} as a decoder.}\ Stage 2 prepares a state that is
  \emph{exactly the QFT of the basis state} $\Ket{f}$ where $f = \varphi\cdot 2^n$.
  Applying the \IQFT{} simply undoes this transform, recovering $\Ket{f}$.
  Measuring the phase register then gives $f$, and
  $\tilde\varphi = f/2^n \approx \varphi$.

  This is the quantum analogue of the inverse FFT: Stage 2 prepares a
  frequency-domain signal (a ``spike at frequency $\varphi$''), and the
  \IQFT{} converts it back to the time domain, yielding the basis state
  $\Ket{f}$.

  \medskip
  \textbf{Why \IQFT{} and not QFT?}\ Because Stage 2 prepares $\QFT\Ket{f}$,
  not $\Ket{f}$ itself.  We want to go backward through the QFT, recovering
  $\Ket{f}$ from its QFT image.  That backward step is the \emph{inverse}
  QFT.  Applying the forward QFT again would produce
  $\QFT(\QFT\Ket{f})$, which is a (bit-reversed) permutation of $\Ket{f}$
  — not the sharp recovery we want.
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[warning]
  This exact analysis requires $\varphi = f/2^n$ for an integer $f$, i.e.\
  $\varphi$ is an \emph{exact $n$-bit binary fraction}.  When this condition
  holds, \QPE{} succeeds with probability 1.  When it does not, we obtain a
  probability distribution peaked near $\lfloor \varphi \cdot 2^n \rceil$
  (the nearest integer), and the success probability has a lower bound
  of $4/\pi^2 \approx 40.5\%$.  Section~\ref{sec:precision} analyses this
  in full detail.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Complete Step-by-Step Circuit Analysis}
\label{sec:derivation}

We now trace the circuit \emph{algebraically}, step by step, for a general
$n$-qubit phase register.

\subsection{Initial State}

\[
  \Ket{\Psi_0} = \underbrace{\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}}_{\text{phase register}} \otimes
  \underbrace{\Ket{\psi}}_{\text{eigenstate}}.
\]

\subsection{After the Hadamard Layer}

Apply $H$ to each of the $n$ phase qubits independently:
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_1}
  = H^{\otimes n}\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}\otimes\Ket{\psi}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1}\Ket{j}\otimes\Ket{\psi}.
\]

\subsection{After the Controlled-\texorpdfstring{$U^{2^k}$}{U^{}2^{}k} Gates}

\begin{proposition}{}{}
  After applying controlled-$U^{2^k}$ (control: ancilla qubit $k$, target:
  eigenstate register) for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$, the state is:
  \[
    \Ket{\Psi_2}
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii \varphi j}\Ket{j}\otimes\Ket{\psi}.
  \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Write the phase register in the tensor product basis as
  $\Ket{j} = \Ket{j_{n-1}}\otimes\cdots\otimes\Ket{j_0}$.
  Qubit $k$ is in the state $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\Ket{0}+\Ket{1})$.
  Applying controlled-$U^{2^k}$ to qubit $k$ and the eigenstate register:
  \[
    \frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\otimes\Ket{\psi}
    \;\longmapsto\;
    \frac{\Ket{0}\!\otimes\!\Ket{\psi}+\Ket{1}\!\otimes\! U^{2^k}\Ket{\psi}}{\sqrt{2}}
    = \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii \varphi 2^k}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\otimes\Ket{\psi}.
  \]
  The second equality uses $U^{2^k}\Ket{\psi} = (e^{2\pi\ii\varphi})^{2^k}\Ket{\psi}
  = e^{2\pi\ii\varphi 2^k}\Ket{\psi}$, which holds because $\Ket{\psi}$ is an
  eigenstate of $U$ with eigenvalue $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi}$.
  Different controlled gates act on different (non-overlapping) control qubits
  but the same eigenstate register; since each leaves $\Ket{\psi}$ invariant,
  they act independently, and the phase register after all $n$ gates is:
  \[
    \Ket{\Psi_2}
    = \bigotimes_{k=0}^{n-1}
        \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi \ii \varphi 2^k}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
      \otimes\Ket{\psi}.
  \]
  Expanding the tensor product and noting that the coefficient of
  $\Ket{j} = \Ket{j_{n-1}\cdots j_0}$ is
  \[
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}
    \prod_{k=0}^{n-1} \left(e^{2\pi \ii \varphi 2^k}\right)^{j_k}
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}} e^{2\pi \ii \varphi \sum_k 2^k j_k}
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}} e^{2\pi \ii \varphi j},
  \]
  the result follows. \qedhere
\end{proof}

\subsection{After the Inverse QFT}

We apply \IQFT{} to the phase register.  By definition:
\[
  \IQFT\Ket{j} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} e^{-2\pi \ii jk/2^n}\Ket{k}.
\]
By linearity, applying \IQFT{} to $\Ket{\Psi_2}$:
\begin{align}
  \Ket{\Psi_3}
  &= \IQFT\left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii \varphi j}\Ket{j}\right]
     \otimes\Ket{\psi} \notag \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii \varphi j}
       \left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} e^{-2\pi \ii jk/2^n}\Ket{k}\right]
     \otimes\Ket{\psi} \notag \\
  &= \frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1}\left[
       \sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii j(\varphi - k/2^n)}
     \right]\Ket{k}\otimes\Ket{\psi}.
  \label{eq:after_iqft}
\end{align}

\begin{definition}{Probability Amplitude and Measurement Probability}{}
  From Eq.~\eqref{eq:after_iqft}, the \textbf{probability amplitude} of
  measuring outcome $k \in \{0, \ldots, 2^n-1\}$ in the phase register is:
  \[
    \alpha_k = \frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii j(\varphi - k/2^n)},
  \]
  and the corresponding \textbf{measurement probability} is $P(k) = |\alpha_k|^2$.
  Note that $\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} P(k) = 1$ (probability normalisation), which
  follows from the unitarity of \IQFT.
\end{definition}

\medskip

\subsubsection{Case 1: $\varphi$ is an exact $n$-bit binary fraction}

Suppose $\varphi = f/2^n$ for some integer $f \in \{0,\ldots,2^n-1\}$.  Then:
\[
  \alpha_k = \frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi \ii j(f-k)/2^n}.
\]
This is a geometric series with common ratio $r = e^{2\pi\ii(f-k)/2^n}$:
\[
  \sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1} r^j =
  \begin{cases}
    2^n & r = 1, \text{ i.e., } f = k \pmod{2^n}, \\
    \dfrac{1 - r^{2^n}}{1 - r} = \dfrac{1 - e^{2\pi\ii(f-k)}}{1 - e^{2\pi\ii(f-k)/2^n}} = 0
      & r \neq 1.
  \end{cases}
\]
The numerator vanishes for $r\neq 1$ because $f-k$ is a non-zero integer,
making $e^{2\pi\ii(f-k)} = 1$.  Therefore $\alpha_k = \delta_{kf}$, and:
\[
  \boxed{P(k) =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & k = f, \\
    0 & k \neq f.
  \end{cases}}
\]
Measuring gives $f$ with certainty, and we recover $\varphi = f/2^n$ exactly.

\subsubsection{Case 2: $\varphi$ is not exactly representable}

Let $b = \lfloor \varphi \cdot 2^n \rceil$ be the nearest integer to $\varphi\cdot 2^n$
(ties broken arbitrarily).  Write the deviation
$\delta = \varphi - b/2^n \in (-1/2^{n+1}, 1/2^{n+1}]$.  Then, using the
geometric series formula and the identity $|1 - e^{i\theta}| = 2|\sin(\theta/2)|$:
\[
  P(b)
  = \frac{1}{4^n}\left|\sum_{j=0}^{2^n-1}e^{2\pi \ii j\delta}\right|^2
  = \frac{1}{4^n}\cdot\frac{\sin^2(\pi 2^n \delta)}{\sin^2(\pi\delta)}.
\]
For $\delta = 0$ (exact case) this equals $1$.  To find the worst-case
lower bound, we maximise $|\delta|$ subject to $|\delta| \leq 1/2^{n+1}$:
\[
  P(b) \;\geq\; \frac{1}{4^n}\cdot\frac{1}{\sin^2(\pi/2^{n+1})}\cdot\sin^2\!\left(\frac{\pi\cdot 2^n}{2^{n+1}}\right)
  = \frac{\sin^2(\pi/2)}{\bigl(2^n \sin(\pi/2^{n+1})\bigr)^2}
  = \frac{1}{\bigl(2^n \sin(\pi/2^{n+1})\bigr)^2}.
\]
Using $\sin x \leq x$ gives $2^n\sin(\pi/2^{n+1}) \leq 2^n \cdot \pi/2^{n+1} = \pi/2$, so:
\[
  P(b) \;\geq\; \frac{1}{(\pi/2)^2} = \frac{4}{\pi^2} \approx 0.405.
\]

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  Even in the worst case (phase exactly halfway between two representable
  values), \QPE{} still succeeds with probability at least $40.5\%$.
  Repeating the algorithm $r$ times and taking the median (or majority
  vote) increases the success probability to $1-\epsilon$ for any $\epsilon>0$,
  at a cost of $\bigO(\log(1/\epsilon))$ repetitions (by a Chernoff bound).
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Precision, Success Probability, and Error Analysis}
\label{sec:precision}

\subsection{Precision of \texorpdfstring{$n$}{n} Ancilla Qubits}

With $n$ ancilla qubits, the phase register can represent $2^n$ distinct
values: $\{0, 1/2^n, 2/2^n, \ldots, (2^n-1)/2^n\}$.  The \emph{resolution}
(smallest gap between representable values) is $1/2^n$.

\begin{theorem}{QPE Precision Guarantee}{}
  Let $U$ be a unitary with eigenstate $\Ket{\psi}$ and eigenphase
  $\varphi\in[0,1)$.  Let $\epsilon, \epsilon' \in (0,1)$ be desired
  parameters.  With
  \[
    n = \left\lceil\log_2\!\left(\frac{2}{\epsilon}\right)\right\rceil
    + \left\lceil\log_2\!\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon'}\right)\right\rceil
    \quad\text{ancilla qubits},
  \]
  the \QPE{} circuit outputs an estimate $\tilde\varphi = k/2^n$ satisfying
  \[
    |\tilde\varphi - \varphi| \leq \epsilon'
  \]
  with probability at least $1 - \epsilon$.
\end{theorem}

\medskip

\textbf{Simple rule of thumb:} to estimate $\varphi$ to $t$ bits of precision
with success probability at least $1 - 2^{-(s)}$ (for some safety margin
$s \geq 1$), use $n = t + s$ ancilla qubits.  Each additional qubit beyond $t$
exponentially suppresses the failure probability.

  number of calls to $U$.

\section{Fully Worked Numerical Example: The \texorpdfstring{$T$}{T} Gate (\texorpdfstring{$n=3$}{n=3} Qubits)}
\label{sec:example}

We now execute the \emph{complete} \QPE{} circuit for the running example
from Section~\ref{subsec:running}, tracking every quantum state explicitly.

\begin{tcolorbox}[example, title={Setup Recap}]
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Unitary: $U = T = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{\ii\pi/4}\end{pmatrix}$
    \item Eigenstate: $\Ket{\psi} = \Ket{1}$
    \item Eigenvalue: $T\Ket{1} = e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{1}$, \
          so $\lambda = e^{2\pi\ii/8}$, hence $\varphi = 1/8$
    \item Number of ancilla qubits: $n = 3$
    \item Phase register qubits: $q_2$ (MSB), $q_1$, $q_0$ (LSB)
    \item Binary representation: $\varphi = 1/8 = 0.001_2$
    \item Expected outcome: integer $f=1$, i.e.\ string $001$,
          giving $\tilde\varphi = 1/8$ with probability 1
  \end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Step 0: Powers of \texorpdfstring{$T$}{T} and Their Action on \texorpdfstring{$\Ket{1}$}{|1>}}

We need $T^{2^0} = T$, $T^{2^1} = T^2$, and $T^{2^2} = T^4$.  Computing:

\[
  T = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{\ii\pi/4}\end{pmatrix},
  \qquad
  T^2 = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{\ii\pi/2}\end{pmatrix}
       = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&\ii\end{pmatrix}
       \;(= S \text{ gate}),
  \qquad
  T^4 = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{\ii\pi}\end{pmatrix}
       = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}
       \;(= Z \text{ gate}).
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Verification using phase kickback formula.}\ For any $k \in \{0,1,2\}$:
\[
  T^{2^k}\Ket{1}
  = e^{\ii\pi 2^k/4}\Ket{1}
  = e^{2\pi \ii \cdot 2^k/8}\Ket{1},
\]
so phase kickback on ancilla $k$ will deposit the factor $e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 2^k/8}$
onto the $\Ket{1}$ branch of that qubit.  Explicitly:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}cccc@{}}
\toprule
Qubit & Gate applied & Action on $\Ket{1}$ & Phase deposited \\
\midrule
$q_0$ (LSB) & $T^{2^0}=T$ & $e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{1}$ & $e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 1/8}$ \\
$q_1$       & $T^{2^1}=S$ & $\ii\Ket{1}$ & $e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 2/8}$ \\
$q_2$ (MSB) & $T^{2^2}=Z$ & $-\Ket{1}$ & $e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 4/8}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Step 1: Initial State}

\[
  \Ket{\Psi_0} = \Ket{0}_{q_2}\Ket{0}_{q_1}\Ket{0}_{q_0}\otimes\Ket{1}
  = \Ket{000}\otimes\Ket{1}.
\]

\subsection{Step 2: Apply Hadamard to Each Ancilla}

Recall $H\Ket{0} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\Ket{0}+\Ket{1})$.  Applying $H$ to each qubit:
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_1}
  = \frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\otimes
    \frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\otimes
    \frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\otimes\Ket{1}.
\]
Expanding all $2^3 = 8$ terms of the phase register:
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_1}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\bigl(
    \Ket{000}+\Ket{001}+\Ket{010}+\Ket{011}
    +\Ket{100}+\Ket{101}+\Ket{110}+\Ket{111}
  \bigr)\otimes\Ket{1}.
\]

\subsection{Step 3: Apply Controlled-\texorpdfstring{$T^4$}{T\^{}4} (\texorpdfstring{$q_2$}{q2} controls \texorpdfstring{$Z$}{Z})}

When $q_2 = 1$, apply $Z$ to the eigenstate register.
\[
  Z\Ket{1} = -\Ket{1} = e^{\ii\pi}\Ket{1} = e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 4/8}\Ket{1}.
\]
Phase kickback: the phase $-1 = e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 4/8}$ appears on the $\Ket{1}$
component of $q_2$.  Only $q_2$ is affected; $q_1$ and $q_0$ are unchanged:
\[
  q_2:\;\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \;\longmapsto\;
  \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 4/8}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  = \frac{\Ket{0}-\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}.
\]

After this gate:
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_{2a}}
  = \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}-\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_2}
    \otimes
    \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_1}
    \otimes
    \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_0}
    \otimes\Ket{1}.
\]

\subsection{Step 4: Apply Controlled-\texorpdfstring{$T^2$}{T\^{}2} (\texorpdfstring{$q_1$}{q1} controls \texorpdfstring{$S$}{S})}

When $q_1=1$, apply $S$ to the eigenstate register.
\[
  S\Ket{1} = \ii\Ket{1} = e^{\ii\pi/2}\Ket{1} = e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 2/8}\Ket{1}.
\]
Phase kickback on $q_1$:
\[
  q_1:\;\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \;\longmapsto\;
  \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 2/8}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  = \frac{\Ket{0}+\ii\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}.
\]

After this gate ($q_2$ and $q_0$ unchanged):
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_{2b}}
  = \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}-\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_2}
    \otimes
    \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}+\ii\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_1}
    \otimes
    \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_0}
    \otimes\Ket{1}.
\]

\subsection{Step 5: Apply Controlled-\texorpdfstring{$T^1$}{T\^{}1} (\texorpdfstring{$q_0$}{q0} controls \texorpdfstring{$T$}{T})}

When $q_0=1$, apply $T$ to the eigenstate register.
\[
  T\Ket{1} = e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{1} = e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 1/8}\Ket{1}.
\]
Phase kickback on $q_0$:
\[
  q_0:\;\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \;\longmapsto\;
  \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 1/8}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  = \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}.
\]

After all three controlled gates:
\begin{align}
  \Ket{\Psi_2}
  &= \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}-\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_2}
     \otimes
     \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}+\ii\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_1}
     \otimes
     \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}+e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_0}
     \otimes\Ket{1}.
  \label{eq:psi2_product}
\end{align}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Expanding $\Ket{\Psi_2}$ to verify the general formula.}\
The coefficient of $\Ket{j_2 j_1 j_0}$ in the phase register is:
\[
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\cdot(-1)^{j_2}\cdot\ii^{j_1}\cdot e^{\ii\pi j_0/4}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\cdot e^{\ii\pi j_2}\cdot e^{\ii\pi j_1/2}\cdot e^{\ii\pi j_0/4}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\,e^{\ii\pi(4j_2+2j_1+j_0)/4}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\,e^{2\pi\ii j/8},
\]
where $j = 4j_2+2j_1+j_0$.  This confirms Eq.~\eqref{eq:after_controlled}
with $\varphi = 1/8$:
\begin{align}
  \Ket{\Psi_2}
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\sum_{j=0}^{7} e^{2\pi\ii j/8}\Ket{j}\otimes\Ket{1} \notag \\
  &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\bigl(
    \Ket{0}
    + e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{1}
    + e^{\ii\pi/2}\Ket{2}
    + e^{3\ii\pi/4}\Ket{3}
    + e^{\ii\pi}\Ket{4}
    + e^{5\ii\pi/4}\Ket{5}
    + e^{3\ii\pi/2}\Ket{6}
    + e^{7\ii\pi/4}\Ket{7}
  \bigr)\otimes\Ket{1}.
  \label{eq:psi2_expanded}
\end{align}
Written with explicit binary strings (phase register only):
\begin{align*}
  \Ket{\Psi_2}\big|_\text{phase}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\bigl(
    &\phantom{+}\Ket{000}
    + e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{001}
    + \ii\Ket{010}
    + \ii e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{011} \\
    &- \Ket{100}
    - e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{101}
    - \ii\Ket{110}
    - \ii e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{111}
  \bigr).
\end{align*}

\subsection{Step 6: Apply the 3-Qubit Inverse QFT}
\label{subsec:iqft_application}

We apply $\IQFT$ to the phase register state $\Ket{\Phi} =
\frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\sum_{j=0}^{7} e^{2\pi\ii j/8}\Ket{j}$.

Using the \IQFT{} formula from Eq.~\eqref{eq:after_iqft} with $\varphi = 1/8$:
\begin{align*}
  \Ket{\Psi_3}\big|_\text{phase}
  &= \frac{1}{8}\sum_{k=0}^{7}\left[\sum_{j=0}^{7} e^{2\pi\ii j(1/8-k/8)}\right]\Ket{k}
   = \frac{1}{8}\sum_{k=0}^{7}\left[\sum_{j=0}^{7} e^{2\pi\ii j(1-k)/8}\right]\Ket{k}.
\end{align*}
Let $\omega = e^{2\pi\ii/8}$ be the primitive 8th root of unity.
The inner sum is $S_k = \sum_{j=0}^{7}\omega^{j(1-k)}$.  Using the geometric
series formula:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccc@{}}
\toprule
$k$ & $1-k \pmod{8}$ & $S_k = \sum_{j=0}^7 \omega^{j(1-k)}$ \\
\midrule
$0$ & $1$ & $\frac{1-\omega^8}{1-\omega} = 0$ \quad ($\omega^8=1$, $\omega\neq 1$)\\
$1$ & $0$ & $\sum_{j=0}^7 1 = 8$ \\
$2$ & $-1\equiv 7$ & $\frac{1-\omega^{-8}}{1-\omega^{-1}} = 0$ \\
$3,\ldots,7$ & nonzero & $0$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Only $k=1$ gives a non-zero sum, so:
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_3}\big|_\text{phase} = \frac{1}{8}\cdot 8\cdot\Ket{1} = \Ket{1} = \Ket{001}.
\]

Therefore:
\[
  \boxed{\Ket{\Psi_3} = \Ket{001}\otimes\Ket{1}.}
\]

\subsection{Step 7: Measurement and Reading the Result}

Measuring the phase register yields the string $\mathbf{001}$ with probability $|\langle 001 | \Psi_3\rangle|^2 = 1$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Reading off $\tilde\varphi$:}\ Interpret the measured integer
$k=1$ as a binary fraction:
\[
  \tilde\varphi = \frac{k}{2^n} = \frac{1}{2^3} = \frac{1}{8} = 0.001_2.
\]
The eigenstate register is left in the state $\Ket{1}$, undisturbed throughout.

\begin{tcolorbox}[example, title={Result}]
  \[
    \boxed{\tilde\varphi = \frac{1}{8} = \varphi \;\checkmark}
  \]
  \QPE{} recovers the phase \textbf{exactly} because $\varphi = 1/8$ is
  representable as a 3-bit binary fraction.  The measurement outcome
  $\Ket{001}$ occurs with probability 1.  The eigenstate $\Ket{1}$
  is returned intact.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Connection to the QFT Product Formula}
\label{subsec:qft_connection}

We can also verify the result by checking that $\Ket{\Psi_2}|_\text{phase}$ is exactly $\QFT\Ket{001}$, which would directly imply $\IQFT\Ket{\Psi_2}|_\text{phase} = \Ket{001}$.

Using the QFT definition with $j=1$ (i.e.\ the state $\Ket{001} = \Ket{j_2=0,j_1=0,j_0=1}$):
\[
  \QFT\Ket{1}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\sum_{k=0}^{7}e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 1\cdot k/8}\Ket{k}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\sum_{k=0}^{7}e^{2\pi\ii k/8}\Ket{k},
\]
which matches Eq.~\eqref{eq:psi2_expanded} exactly.
\QFT{} can also be verified via the product formula with
$j_1=j_2=0, j_3=1$ ($n=3$, bits indexed $j_1j_2j_3$ from MSB):
\[
  \QFT\Ket{001}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 0.1}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
    \otimes
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 0.01}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
    \otimes
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 0.001}\Ket{1}\Bigr),
\]
where $0.1_2 = 1/2$, $0.01_2 = 1/4$, $0.001_2 = 1/8$, giving:
\[
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{\ii\pi}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
    \otimes
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{\ii\pi/2}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
    \otimes
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}-\Ket{1}\Bigr)
    \otimes
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}+\ii\Ket{1}\Bigr)
    \otimes
    \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{\ii\pi/4}\Ket{1}\Bigr),
\]
which matches Eq.~\eqref{eq:psi2_product} exactly.  This confirms the
theoretical prediction: Stage 2 produces $\QFT\Ket{f}$ and Stage 3 undoes
it via the \IQFT, recovering $\Ket{f} = \Ket{001}$.

\section{Non-Exact Phase: A Second Worked Example}

We now analyse what happens when $\varphi$ is \emph{not} exactly representable.
Take $U$ to be some unitary with $\varphi = 1/3$ and $n=3$ ancilla qubits.

\subsection{After the Controlled Gates}

The general formula gives:

\section{The Inverse QFT Circuit}
\label{sec:iqft}

The QFT is itself a quantum algorithm.  Since \QPE{} uses the \IQFT, we must
understand its structure.

\subsection{The QFT Circuit Gates}

The $n$-qubit QFT uses:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $n$ Hadamard gates $H$.
  \item $\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ controlled phase gates
        $R_m = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{2\pi\ii/2^m}\end{pmatrix}$
        for $m = 2, 3, \ldots, n$, where $R_m$ adds phase $e^{2\pi\ii/2^m}$
        when the control qubit is $\Ket{1}$.
  \item $\lfloor n/2\rfloor$ SWAP gates (to reverse bit order at the output).
\end{itemize}

\[\begin{quantikz}[row sep=0.4cm, column sep=0.6cm]
\lstick{$\ket{j_1}$} & \gate{H} & \gate{R_2} & \gate{R_3} & \qw & \cdots & & \gate{R_n} & \qw & \swap{3} & \qw \\
\lstick{$\ket{j_2}$} & \qw & \ctrl{-1} & \qw & \gate{H} & \cdots & & \gate{R_{n-1}} & \qw & \qw & \qw \\
\lstick{\vdots} & & & & & \ddots & & & & & \\
\lstick{$\ket{j_n}$} & \qw & \qw & \qw & \qw & \cdots & & \ctrl{-3} & \gate{H} & \targX{} & \qw
\end{quantikz}\]

The \IQFT{} is the \emph{time-reversed circuit with all phase angles negated}:
replace each $R_m$ by
$R_m^\dagger = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{-2\pi\ii/2^m}\end{pmatrix}$
and run the gates in reverse order.

\subsection{Why Does the QFT Circuit Work?}

The QFT maps $\Ket{j_1 j_2 \cdots j_n}$ to the product form
\[
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}
  \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\, 0.j_n}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
  \otimes
  \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\, 0.j_{n-1}j_n}\Ket{1}\Bigr)
  \otimes\cdots\otimes
  \Bigl(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\, 0.j_1 j_2\cdots j_n}\Ket{1}\Bigr).
\]
The circuit builds this factor by factor:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{$H$ on qubit 1:}
    $\Ket{j_1} \mapsto \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\Ket{0}+(-1)^{j_1}\Ket{1})
    = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 0.j_1}\Ket{1})$.
  \item \textbf{Controlled-$R_2$ from qubit 2:}
    multiplies the $\Ket{1}$ component by $e^{2\pi\ii j_2/4}$ when qubit 2 is in $\Ket{1}$,
    giving $e^{2\pi\ii\, 0.j_1 j_2}$ in the exponent.
  \item \textbf{Successive controlled-$R_m$ from qubit $m$:}
    each appends one more bit to the binary fraction in the exponent.
  \item \textbf{Repeat for each qubit}, then \textbf{SWAP} to reverse bit order.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{QFT Verification: 3-Qubit QFT of \texorpdfstring{$\Ket{001}$}{|001>}}
\label{subsec:qft_example}

We verify that $\QFT\Ket{001}$ matches $\Ket{\Psi_2}|_\text{phase}$ from
our example (Section~\ref{subsec:qft_connection}), confirming that the \IQFT{}
recovers $\Ket{001}$ exactly.

For $\Ket{001}$: $j_1=0$, $j_2=0$, $j_3=1$, so $j = 1$.  By definition:
\[
  \QFT\Ket{001}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\sum_{k=0}^{7}e^{2\pi\ii\cdot k/8}\Ket{k},
\]
which matches $\Ket{\Psi_2}|_\text{phase}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:psi2_expanded}. $\checkmark$

\section{QPE With a General (Mixed Eigenstate) Input}
\label{sec:general}

So far we assumed $\Ket{\psi}$ is a \emph{pure eigenstate}.  In practice,
one often can only prepare a state with \emph{partial overlap} with the
desired eigenstate.  We now analyse this case.

\subsection{Superposition of Eigenstates}

Let $\{U\Ket{u_j} = e^{2\pi\ii\varphi_j}\Ket{u_j}\}_{j=0,1,\ldots}$ be the
full set of eigenvalue/eigenstate pairs of $U$.  Suppose the eigenstate
register is initialised to:
\[
  \Ket{\chi} = \sum_j c_j \Ket{u_j}, \quad \sum_j |c_j|^2 = 1.
\]
By linearity of the \QPE{} circuit:
\[
  \Ket{0}^{\otimes n}\otimes\Ket{\chi}
  = \sum_j c_j \,\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}\otimes\Ket{u_j}
  \;\;\xrightarrow{\text{QPE}}\;\;
  \sum_j c_j \,\Ket{\tilde\varphi_j}\otimes\Ket{u_j},
\]
where $\Ket{\tilde\varphi_j}$ is the phase-register state for eigenphase
$\varphi_j$ (a peak centred at $\lfloor\varphi_j\cdot 2^n\rceil$).

\subsection{Measurement Outcomes}

Measuring the phase register:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item With probability $|c_j|^2$, we measure (approximately) $\varphi_j$
        and the eigenstate register collapses to $\Ket{u_j}$.
  \item Each run of \QPE{} \emph{samples} one eigenphase, weighted by $|c_j|^2$.
  \item If one eigenstate dominates ($|c_0|^2 \approx 1$), \QPE{} reliably
        returns $\varphi_0$ with high probability.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  This mechanism is used in the \textbf{Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE)}
  and quantum chemistry applications: one prepares a trial state $\Ket{\chi}$
  with large overlap $|c_0|^2$ with the ground state $\Ket{u_0}$ of the
  Hamiltonian $H$, then runs \QPE{} on $U = e^{-\ii H t}$ to estimate the
  ground-state energy $E_0 = 2\pi\varphi_0/t$.  The better the trial state,
  the more reliably \QPE{} returns the ground-state energy.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Applications of Quantum Phase Estimation}
\label{sec:applications}

\QPE{} is arguably the most important quantum subroutine.  Here we survey
its most significant applications.

\subsection{Shor's Algorithm for Integer Factorisation}

Shor's algorithm~\cite{shor1994} factors an integer $N$ in polynomial time.
The key step: find the \emph{period} $r$ of the function $f(x) = a^x \bmod N$.
This is done by constructing the unitary
\[
  U_a\Ket{x} = \Ket{ax \bmod N}
\]
and noting that the eigenstates of $U_a$ are
$\Ket{u_s} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\sum_{k=0}^{r-1}e^{-2\pi\ii sk/r}\Ket{a^k\bmod N}$
with eigenvalues $e^{2\pi\ii s/r}$ for $s = 0, 1, \ldots, r-1$.
\QPE{} extracts these eigenvalues; from $s/r$ (as a fraction), $r$ is
recovered via the continued-fraction algorithm.  Factoring then follows from
$r$ using classical number theory.  Overall complexity:
$\bigO((\log N)^3)$ vs.\ $\bigO\!\left(\exp((\log N)^{1/3}(\log\log N)^{2/3})\right)$
for the best classical algorithm.

\subsection{Quantum Simulation}

Given a Hamiltonian $H$, the time-evolution operator $U = e^{-\ii Ht}$ is
unitary with eigenvalues $e^{-\ii E_k t}$, where $E_k$ are energy eigenvalues.
\QPE{} on $U$ extracts the energy spectrum, enabling:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Molecular ground-state energies (quantum chemistry / drug discovery).
  \item Condensed-matter band structures and phase transitions.
  \item Lattice gauge theory simulations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{HHL Algorithm for Linear Systems}

The Harrow--Hassidim--Lloyd (HHL) algorithm~\cite{harrow2009} solves
$A\vec{x}=\vec{b}$ for a sparse Hermitian $A$ in time
$\bigO(\log N \cdot \kappa^2 s^2 / \epsilon)$ (where $\kappa$ is the condition
number and $s$ the sparsity), vs.\ $\bigO(N\kappa s)$ classically.  The
core subroutine is \QPE{} applied to $e^{\ii At}$, which extracts the
eigenvalues $\lambda_j$ of $A$; a rotation then applies $1/\lambda_j$,
implementing $A^{-1}$ in superposition.

\subsection{Quantum Counting}

\QPE{} applied to the Grover iterator $G$ extracts $\theta$ where
$\sin^2\theta = M/N$ ($M$ = number of solutions, $N$ = search space size).
From $\theta$, $M$ is computed exactly.  This gives a quadratic speedup
in counting solutions compared to classical sampling.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Summary of QPE applications.  In each case, the phase encodes the
  quantity of interest, and extracting the phase solves the underlying problem.}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Application} & \textbf{Unitary $U$} & \textbf{Phase encodes} \\
\midrule
Shor's factoring & $U_a\Ket{x}=\Ket{ax\bmod N}$ & Period $r$ of $a^x\bmod N$ \\
Quantum chemistry & $e^{-\ii Ht}$ & Energy eigenvalue $E_k$ \\
HHL linear systems & $e^{\ii At}$ & Eigenvalue $\lambda_j$ of $A$ \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\section{Practical Considerations for QPE}
\label{sec:practical}

\subsection{Implementing Controlled-\texorpdfstring{$U^{2^k}$}{U\^{}2\^{}k} in Practice}

The main cost of \QPE{} comes from the controlled-$U^{2^k}$ gates.  For large
$k$, $2^k$ is exponentially large.  Two strategies:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\arabic*.}, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Repeated squaring.}\ Compute $U^{2^k}$ by squaring $k$ times:
        $U^2 = U\cdot U$, $U^4 = (U^2)^2$, etc.  Cost: $\bigO(n)$
        matrix multiplications (or circuit compositions) to get all powers.
  \item \textbf{Problem-specific structure.}\ For quantum simulation,
        $e^{-\ii H\cdot 2^k t} = e^{-\ii H(2^k t)}$.  This can be implemented
        using Trotter--Suzuki decompositions applied to the longer time $2^k t$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Ancilla Qubit Count vs.\ Precision}

The trade-off is fundamental:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Each additional ancilla qubit \emph{doubles} the precision
        (resolution $1/2^n$).
  \item For chemistry applications, ``chemical accuracy'' requires
        $|\tilde\varphi - \varphi| \lesssim 10^{-3}$, demanding at least
        $\lceil\log_2(10^3)\rceil = 10$ ancilla qubits.
  \item Each ancilla qubit adds one layer of controlled gates, increasing
        circuit depth linearly in $n$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Noise and Error Mitigation on NISQ Devices}

On current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices, every gate has
error rate $\epsilon_g \sim 10^{-3}$ to $10^{-2}$.  A \QPE{} circuit with
$\bigO(n^2)$ gates accumulates total error $\sim n^2\epsilon_g$, which grows
quickly with precision.  Mitigation strategies include:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Iterative QPE (IQPE):} Estimate one bit of $\varphi$ at a time
        using a single ancilla qubit, iterating $n$ times.
        Reduces the maximum circuit depth from $\bigO(n^2)$ to $\bigO(n)$
        at the cost of $n$ sequential measurements.
  \item \textbf{Hadamard test:} Estimate $\operatorname{Re}\Bra{\psi}U^k\Ket{\psi}$
        and $\operatorname{Im}\Bra{\psi}U^k\Ket{\psi}$ separately using
        single-ancilla circuits, then use classical post-processing
        to recover $\varphi$.
  \item \textbf{Error-mitigation techniques:} Zero-noise extrapolation,
        probabilistic error cancellation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Iterative QPE (IQPE)}
\label{subsec:iqpe}

IQPE estimates $\varphi = 0.\omega_1\omega_2\cdots\omega_n$ one bit at a time,
using only \emph{one ancilla qubit}.  The idea: the LSB of $2^{n-1}\varphi$
(in binary) is revealed by measuring the ancilla after applying controlled-$U^{2^{n-1}}$.
Classical feedback then corrects for the already-extracted bits before
estimating the next.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Iterative QPE (IQPE)}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Oracle for controlled-$U$; eigenstate $\Ket{\psi}$; desired bits $n$
\Ensure $n$-bit estimate $\tilde\varphi = 0.\omega_1\omega_2\cdots\omega_n$
\State $\Phi \gets 0$ \Comment{Accumulated classical feedback angle}
\For{$k = n, n-1, \ldots, 1$}
  \State Prepare ancilla: $\Ket{+} \gets H\Ket{0}$ \Comment{Create superposition}
  \State Apply controlled-$U^{2^{k-1}}$ to $\Ket{\psi}$ with ancilla as control \Comment{Phase kickback}
  \State Apply $R_Z(-\Phi)$ to ancilla \Comment{Undo accumulated phase from prior bits}
  \State Apply $H$ to ancilla \Comment{Interference to read out bit}
  \State Measure ancilla; store result as $\omega_{n-k+1} \in \{0,1\}$
  \State $\Phi \gets (\Phi + \omega_{n-k+1}\cdot\pi) / 2$ \Comment{Update feedback for next round}
\EndFor
\State \Return $\tilde\varphi = \sum_{k=1}^n \omega_k / 2^k$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Complete Algorithm Summary}
\label{sec:summary}

\begin{tcolorbox}[summary]
  \textbf{Quantum Phase Estimation — Complete Description}

  \medskip
  \textbf{Input:} Black-box unitary $U$; (approximate) eigenstate $\Ket{\psi}$
  with $U\Ket{\psi}=e^{2\pi\ii\varphi}\Ket{\psi}$; desired precision $n$ bits.

  \medskip
  \textbf{Output:} $\tilde\varphi \in \{0, 1/2^n, \ldots, (2^n-1)/2^n\}$
  such that $|\tilde\varphi - \varphi| \leq 1/2^n$ with probability $\geq 4/\pi^2$.

  \medskip
  \textbf{Algorithm:}
  \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Initialise:} $\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}\otimes\Ket{\psi}$.
    \item \textbf{Superposition:} Apply $H^{\otimes n}$ to the phase register.
    \item \textbf{Kickback:} For $k=0,\ldots,n-1$: apply controlled-$U^{2^k}$
          (control: ancilla qubit $k$, target: eigenstate register).
    \item \textbf{Decode:} Apply $\IQFT$ to the phase register.
    \item \textbf{Measure:} Measure the phase register; output
          $\tilde\varphi = (\text{result})/2^n$.
  \end{enumerate}

  \medskip
  \textbf{Why each step:}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Step 2 creates superposition so phase kickback can act on all $\Ket{j}$
          simultaneously.
    \item Step 3 encodes the phase $\varphi$ into the exponentials
          $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi j}$, producing $\QFT\Ket{\varphi\cdot 2^n}$.
    \item Step 4 inverts the QFT, converting the frequency-domain state
          back to the computational basis state $\Ket{f}$ with $f \approx \varphi\cdot 2^n$.
    \item Step 5 reads out $f$ and recovers $\tilde\varphi = f/2^n$.
  \end{itemize}

  \medskip
  \textbf{Cost:}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item $n + \bigO(n^2)$ elementary gates (Hadamards + \IQFT).
    \item $\bigO(n)$ queries to controlled-$U$ (using repeated squaring).
    \item Single-shot success probability $\geq 4/\pi^2 \approx 40.5\%$.
    \item Success probability $\geq 1-\delta$ using $\bigO(\log(1/\delta))$
          repetitions.
  \end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them}
\label{sec:mistakes}

\begin{tcolorbox}[warning]
  \textbf{Mistake 1: Confusing $\varphi$ with the eigenvalue.}\ The
  eigenvalue is $\lambda = e^{2\pi\ii\varphi}$; \QPE{} estimates $\varphi \in [0,1)$,
  not $\lambda$ itself.  To recover the eigenvalue from the measurement
  result $k$, compute $\lambda \approx e^{2\pi\ii k/2^n}$.
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[warning]
  \textbf{Mistake 2: Wrong bit ordering.}\ There are two common conventions
  for which ancilla qubit is MSB vs.\ LSB.  In this lecture (following
  Nielsen \& Chuang), qubit $q_0$ is the LSB and controls $U^{2^0}$.
  Some texts use the opposite convention.  A circuit built with the wrong
  convention outputs the \emph{bit-reversal} of the correct answer — still a
  valid binary string, so the error is silent and numerically plausible.
  Always check: the qubit controlling the \emph{lowest} power $U^1$ must
  be read as the LSB.
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[warning]
  \textbf{Mistake 3: Forgetting the QFT bit-reversal SWAP.}\ The standard
  QFT circuit produces output with bits in \emph{reversed} order relative to
  the input.  The SWAP gates at the end correct this.  If you implement the
  QFT without SWAPs and use it as the forward transform, you must also
  implement the \IQFT{} without SWAPs; they must be \emph{consistent}.
  Mixing the conventions produces a QPE circuit that outputs the bit-reversal
  of $\varphi$, which is a different number.
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[warning]
  \textbf{Mistake 4: Assuming $\Ket{\psi}$ is always an exact eigenstate.}\
  In practice, the input state often has only partial overlap with the target
  eigenstate.  Section~\ref{sec:general} shows that \QPE{} still works, but
  probabilistically samples among the eigenstates with probabilities
  proportional to the squared overlaps $|c_j|^2$.  This is not an error in
  the algorithm; it is the expected behaviour.
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[warning]
  \textbf{Mistake 5: Conflating circuit depth with oracle query count.}\
  \QPE{} requires $\bigO(n)$ controlled-$U$ oracle calls (by squaring).
  But if $U$ has no special structure, each controlled-$U^{2^k}$ may
  require $\bigO(2^k)$ elementary two-qubit gates, so the gate complexity
  can be $\bigO(2^n g)$ in the worst case.  The oracle complexity and gate
  complexity can differ exponentially.  Always state which complexity
  measure you are using.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Review Questions and Exercises}

\subsection{Conceptual Questions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.}, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Phase kickback.}\ Explain in your own words why the phase
        $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi}$ is kicked back onto the \emph{control} qubit
        and not the target qubit.  What property of $\Ket{\psi}$ is essential?
        What happens if the target is not an eigenstate?

  \item \textbf{Why Hadamard first?}\ If we omit the initial Hadamard layer
        and apply the controlled gates to $\Ket{0}^{\otimes n}$, what state
        do we get?  Why does the \IQFT{} then fail to give us $\varphi$?

  \item \textbf{Why powers of 2?}\ Suppose we replace the controlled-$U^{2^k}$
        gates with controlled-$U^k$ (powers $1, 2, 3, \ldots, n$).  Does the
        \IQFT{} still decode the phase?  Explain why not.

  \item \textbf{Precision vs.\ qubits.}\ How many ancilla qubits are needed to
        estimate a phase to within $10^{-6}$ with success probability at
        least $99\%$?  Show your work.

  \item \textbf{The inverse QFT.}\ Why do we apply the \emph{inverse} QFT
        rather than the forward QFT?  What would the phase register state
        be after applying the forward QFT instead of the \IQFT{}?
        (\emph{Hint:} what is $\QFT\circ\QFT\Ket{f}$?)
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Computational Exercises}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{E\arabic*.}, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{$Z$ gate.}\ Run \QPE{} on $U=Z$ with eigenstate $\Ket{1}$
        using $n=2$ ancilla qubits.  Trace all states explicitly (as in
        Section~\ref{sec:example}), verify the measurement outcome, and
        extract $\varphi$.  (Answer: $\varphi = 1/2$, outcome $\Ket{10}$.)

  \item \textbf{$S$ gate.}\ Run \QPE{} on $U=S=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&\ii\end{pmatrix}$
        with $\Ket{\psi}=\Ket{1}$ and $n=3$ qubits.  The eigenvalue is
        $\ii = e^{\ii\pi/2} = e^{2\pi\ii/4}$, so $\varphi=1/4 = 0.010_2$.
        Verify that the measurement outcome is $\Ket{010}$ (i.e., $k=2$,
        $\tilde\varphi = 2/8 = 1/4$).

  \item \textbf{Non-exact phase.}\ Set $\varphi = 1/3$ and $n=3$.  Compute
        $P(k)$ for all $k\in\{0,\ldots,7\}$ using the closed-form formula
        in Appendix~\ref{app:geosum}, and verify that $P(3) \geq 4/\pi^2$.

  \item \textbf{Doubled precision.}\ Repeat Exercise E1 with $n=4$ qubits.
        What is the new resolution?  Does the extra qubit change the
        measurement outcome?  (\emph{Hint:} $1/2 = 8/16$; the outcome should
        be $\Ket{1000}$.)

  \item \textbf{Hadamard test.}\ Show that the circuit:
        (a) $H$ on ancilla, (b) controlled-$U$ with ancilla as control,
        (c) $H$ on ancilla, (d) measure,
        gives $P(\text{ancilla}=0) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\operatorname{Re}\Bra{\psi}U\Ket{\psi})$.
        How is this related to the first step of \QPE{}?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Challenging Problems}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Success probability lower bound.}\ Prove rigorously that
        $P(b) \geq 4/\pi^2$ where $b = \lfloor \varphi\cdot 2^n \rceil$.
        \emph{Hint:} Use the closed form
        $P(b) = \sin^2(\pi 2^n \delta)/(2^n \sin(\pi\delta))^2$
        with $|\delta| \leq 1/2^{n+1}$, then bound
        $2^n|\sin(\pi\delta)| \leq 2^n\cdot |\pi\delta| \leq \pi/2$.

  \item \textbf{Boosting success probability.}\ Given a \QPE{} circuit with
        single-shot success probability $p \geq 4/\pi^2 > 1/3$, show that
        repeating $r$ times and taking the majority vote gives success
        probability $\geq 1 - e^{-\Omega(r)}$.
        (\emph{Hint:} Apply the Chernoff bound to $r$ i.i.d.\ Bernoulli trials
        with bias $p > 1/2$.)

  \item \textbf{Shor's period finding.}\ Verify that the states
        $\Ket{u_s} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\sum_{k=0}^{r-1}e^{-2\pi\ii sk/r}\Ket{a^k\bmod N}$
        are eigenstates of $U_a$ with eigenvalues $e^{2\pi\ii s/r}$.
        Then show that the uniform superposition
        $\sum_{s=0}^{r-1}\Ket{u_s}/\sqrt{r} = \Ket{1}$,
        and explain how this means \QPE{} on $\Ket{1}$ extracts a
        uniformly random $s/r$ (from which $r$ is found via continued fractions).

  \item \textbf{Iterative QPE.}\ Implement the IQPE algorithm
        (Section~\ref{subsec:iqpe}) in a quantum circuit simulator of your
        choice (e.g.\ Qiskit, Cirq, or QuTiP).  Verify it recovers
        $\varphi = 1/8$ exactly using a single ancilla qubit and $n=3$ rounds.
        Also test it on $\varphi = 1/3$ with $n=5$ rounds and report the
        estimate and error.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Key Equations Cheat Sheet}
\label{sec:cheatsheet}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=lightyellow, colframe=qorange, title={\bfseries QPE Quick Reference},
    fonttitle=\bfseries\color{qorange}]

\textbf{Setting:} $U$ unitary; $U\Ket{\psi}=e^{2\pi\ii\varphi}\Ket{\psi}$;
$\varphi\in[0,1)$; $n$ ancilla qubits; $N=2^n$.

\medskip
\textbf{Phase kickback ($k$-th ancilla):}
\[
  c\text{-}U^{2^k}\;\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\Ket{\psi}
  = \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{2\pi\ii\varphi 2^k}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\Ket{\psi}.
\]

\textbf{State after all controlled gates (Stage 2):}
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}e^{2\pi\ii\varphi j}\Ket{j}\otimes\Ket{\psi}.
\]

\textbf{This equals $\QFT\Ket{f}\otimes\Ket{\psi}$} where $f = \varphi N$
(when exact), so \IQFT{} recovers $\Ket{f}$.

\medskip
\textbf{Probability amplitude after \IQFT:}
\[
  \alpha_k = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}e^{2\pi\ii j(\varphi - k/N)}.
\]

\textbf{Probability of outcome $k$:}
\[
  P(k) = |\alpha_k|^2
       = \frac{\sin^2\!\bigl(\pi N(\varphi - k/N)\bigr)}{N^2 \sin^2\!\bigl(\pi(\varphi-k/N)\bigr)}.
\]

\textbf{Exact case} ($\varphi = f/N$, integer $f$): $P(f)=1$, all others $0$.

\textbf{Worst-case success probability:} $P(b) \geq 4/\pi^2 \approx 40.5\%$,
where $b = \lfloor\varphi N\rceil$.

\textbf{Resolution:} $|\tilde\varphi - \varphi| \leq 1/N = 1/2^n$.

\textbf{Gate count:} $\bigO(n^2 + ng)$ total; $\bigO(n)$ queries to $U$.

\medskip
\textbf{QFT:} $\QFT\Ket{j}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}e^{2\pi\ii jk/N}\Ket{k}$.

\textbf{\IQFT:} $\IQFT\Ket{j}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}e^{-2\pi\ii jk/N}\Ket{k}$.

\medskip
\textbf{Binary fraction:} $\varphi = 0.b_1 b_2\cdots b_n = \sum_{k=1}^n b_k/2^k$.

\end{tcolorbox}

\appendix
\section{Geometric Series Closed Form}
\label{app:geosum}

For the probability amplitude computation, we repeatedly use:
\[
  \sum_{j=0}^{N-1} r^j = \frac{1-r^N}{1-r}, \quad r \neq 1, \quad N = 2^n.
\]
With $r = e^{2\pi\ii\alpha}$ for non-integer $\alpha$, using
$|1 - e^{\ii\theta}| = 2|\sin(\theta/2)|$:
\[
  \left|\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}e^{2\pi\ii j\alpha}\right|^2
  = \frac{|1-e^{2\pi\ii N\alpha}|^2}{|1-e^{2\pi\ii\alpha}|^2}
  = \frac{4\sin^2(\pi N\alpha)}{4\sin^2(\pi\alpha)}
  = \frac{\sin^2(\pi N\alpha)}{\sin^2(\pi\alpha)}.
\]
When $\alpha = \varphi - k/N$ (as in the \QPE{} probability computation), this gives:
\[
  P(k) = \frac{1}{N^2}\cdot\frac{\sin^2\!\bigl(\pi N(\varphi - k/N)\bigr)}{\sin^2\!\bigl(\pi(\varphi-k/N)\bigr)}.
\]

\section{Binary Fraction Expansion}
\label{app:binary}

Any $\varphi\in[0,1)$ has the binary expansion
$\varphi = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} b_k 2^{-k}$ with $b_k\in\{0,1\}$.
The $n$-bit truncation $f/2^n = \sum_{k=1}^n b_k 2^{-k}$ satisfies
$|\varphi - f/2^n| \leq 2^{-n}$, with equality when all remaining bits
$b_{n+1}, b_{n+2}, \ldots$ are 1.

\section{Verification of the \texorpdfstring{$T$}{T}-Gate Example by Direct Matrix Computation}
\label{app:matrix}

For completeness, we verify $\IQFT\Ket{\Psi_2}|_\text{phase} = \Ket{001}$
directly using the $8\times 8$ DFT matrix.

The phase-register state before the \IQFT{} is (from Eq.~\eqref{eq:psi2_expanded}):
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_2}\big|_\text{phase}
  = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\bigl[1,\, \omega,\, \omega^2,\, \omega^3,\, \omega^4,\, \omega^5,\, \omega^6,\, \omega^7\bigr]^T,
  \quad \omega = e^{2\pi\ii/8}.
\]
The vector of coefficients is $\vec{v} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}[\omega^j]_{j=0}^7$.

The \IQFT{} matrix has entries $(F^{-1})_{kj} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}e^{-2\pi\ii kj/8} = \frac{\omega^{-kj}}{\sqrt{8}}$.
Then:
\[
  (F^{-1}\vec{v})_k
  = \sum_{j=0}^7 \frac{\omega^{-kj}}{\sqrt{8}} \cdot \frac{\omega^j}{\sqrt{8}}
  = \frac{1}{8}\sum_{j=0}^7 \omega^{j(1-k)}.
\]
By the geometric series formula:
\[
  \frac{1}{8}\sum_{j=0}^7 \omega^{j(1-k)} = \delta_{k,1},
\]
since $\omega^{8} = 1$ (so the sum equals $8$ for $k=1$ and $0$ otherwise).

Therefore $F^{-1}\vec{v} = \vec{e}_1 = [0,1,0,0,0,0,0,0]^T$, corresponding
to the state $\Ket{001}$. $\square$

\section{Second Worked Example: The \texorpdfstring{$S$}{S} Gate with \texorpdfstring{$n=2$}{n=2} Ancilla Qubits}
\label{app:sgate}

This shorter example reinforces every concept from Section~\ref{sec:example}
with a different gate and fewer qubits, so it is easy to check on paper.

\begin{tcolorbox}[example, title={Setup}]
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Unitary: $U = S = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&\ii\end{pmatrix}$
    \item Eigenstate: $\Ket{\psi}=\Ket{1}$, eigenvalue $S\Ket{1}=\ii\Ket{1}=e^{\ii\pi/2}\Ket{1}$
    \item True phase: $\varphi = 1/4 = 0.01_2$
    \item Ancilla qubits: $n=2$, so $N = 4$ representable values: $\{0,\tfrac{1}{4},\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{3}{4}\}$
    \item Phase register: $q_1$ (MSB), $q_0$ (LSB)
    \item Expected outcome: integer $f = 1$, string $\Ket{01}$, giving $\tilde\varphi = 1/4$
  \end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Powers of $S$:}
\[
  S = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&\ii\end{pmatrix},\qquad
  S^2 = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&\ii^2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix} = Z.
\]
Action on the eigenstate:
\[
  S^{2^0}\Ket{1} = \ii\Ket{1} = e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 1/4}\Ket{1},\qquad
  S^{2^1}\Ket{1} = Z\Ket{1} = -\Ket{1} = e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 2/4}\Ket{1}.
\]

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 1 — Initial state:}
$\Ket{\Psi_0} = \Ket{00}\otimes\Ket{1}$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 2 — After $H^{\otimes 2}$:}
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_1}
  = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(\Ket{00}+\Ket{01}+\Ket{10}+\Ket{11}\bigr)\otimes\Ket{1}
  = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=0}^{3}\Ket{j}\otimes\Ket{1}.
\]

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 3 — Apply controlled-$S^2 = Z$ ($q_1$ controls):}

Phase $e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 2/4} = e^{\ii\pi} = -1$ kicks back onto $q_1$:
\[
  q_1:\;\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \;\longmapsto\;
  \frac{\Ket{0}+e^{\ii\pi}\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\Ket{0}-\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}.
\]

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 4 — Apply controlled-$S^1 = S$ ($q_0$ controls):}

Phase $e^{2\pi\ii\cdot 1/4} = \ii$ kicks back onto $q_0$:
\[
  q_0:\;\frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}
  \;\longmapsto\;
  \frac{\Ket{0}+\ii\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}.
\]

State after all controlled gates:
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_2}
  = \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}-\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_1}
    \otimes
    \underbrace{\frac{\Ket{0}+\ii\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}}_{q_0}
    \otimes\Ket{1}.
\]
Expanding (4 terms):
\[
  \Ket{\Psi_2}\big|_\text{phase}
  = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(\Ket{00}+\ii\Ket{01}-\Ket{10}-\ii\Ket{11}\bigr)
  = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(1\cdot\Ket{0}+\ii^1\Ket{1}+\ii^2\Ket{2}+\ii^3\Ket{3}\bigr)
  = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=0}^3 e^{2\pi\ii j/4}\Ket{j}.
\]
This confirms the general formula with $\varphi = 1/4$. $\checkmark$

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 5 — Apply $\IQFT$ to the 2-qubit phase register:}

The 2-qubit \IQFT{} has matrix entries
$(F_4^{-1})_{kj} = \frac{1}{2}e^{-2\pi\ii kj/4}$.  Compute each amplitude:
\[
  \alpha_k = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{j=0}^3 e^{2\pi\ii j(1/4 - k/4)}
           = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{j=0}^3 \ii^{j(1-k)}.
\]
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccl@{}}
\toprule
$k$ & $\sum_{j=0}^3 \ii^{j(1-k)}$ & Value \\
\midrule
0 & $\ii^0+\ii^1+\ii^2+\ii^3 = 1+\ii-1-\ii$ & $= 0$ \\
1 & $\ii^0+\ii^0+\ii^0+\ii^0 = 1+1+1+1$      & $= 4$ \\
2 & $\ii^0+\ii^{-1}+\ii^{-2}+\ii^{-3} = 1-\ii-1+\ii$ & $= 0$ \\
3 & $\ii^0+\ii^{-2}+\ii^{-4}+\ii^{-6} = 1-1+1-1$ & $= 0$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Therefore $\Ket{\Psi_3} = \frac{1}{4}\cdot 4\cdot\Ket{1}\otimes\Ket{1} = \Ket{01}\otimes\Ket{1}$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Measurement:}\ Outcome $\Ket{01}$, i.e.\ $k=1$,
$\tilde\varphi = 1/4$.
\[
  \boxed{\tilde\varphi = \frac{1}{4} = \varphi\;\checkmark}
\]

\section{Shor's Algorithm: Period-Finding via QPE, Step by Step}
\label{app:shor}

We walk through the smallest non-trivial factoring example —
$N=15$, $a=7$ — at full algebraic detail.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

Factor $N = 15$.  Choose $a = 7$ with $\gcd(7,15) = 1$.
We seek the \emph{order} (period) $r$ of $7$ modulo $15$:
the smallest positive integer $r$ such that $7^r \equiv 1 \pmod{15}$.

Computing directly: $7^1=7$, $7^2=49\equiv 4$, $7^3 \equiv 28\equiv 13$,
$7^4 \equiv 91\equiv 1 \pmod{15}$.  So $r = 4$.

\subsection{Constructing the Unitary \texorpdfstring{$U_a$}{Ua}}

Define the unitary $U_7$ acting on a $4$-qubit register
(representing $\{0,1,\ldots,15\}$) by:
\[
  U_7\Ket{x} = \Ket{7x \bmod 15} \quad \text{for } x \in \{1,\ldots,14\},
  \qquad
  U_7\Ket{0} = \Ket{0}.
\]
The orbit of $1$ under repeated application is:
$1 \to 7 \to 4 \to 13 \to 1 \to \cdots$ (period 4).

\subsection{Eigenstates and Eigenvalues of \texorpdfstring{$U_7$}{U7}}

The eigenstates of $U_7$ (restricted to the orbit of 1) are:
\[
  \Ket{u_s} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{3} e^{-2\pi\ii sk/4}\Ket{7^k\bmod 15},
  \quad s = 0,1,2,3.
\]
Explicitly:
\[
  \Ket{u_s} = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(
    \Ket{1} + e^{-2\pi\ii s/4}\Ket{7} + e^{-2\pi\ii s\cdot 2/4}\Ket{4}
    + e^{-2\pi\ii s\cdot 3/4}\Ket{13}
  \bigr).
\]
One can verify $U_7\Ket{u_s} = e^{2\pi\ii s/4}\Ket{u_s}$, so the
eigenvalues are $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi_s}$ with $\varphi_s = s/r = s/4$:
\[
  \varphi_0 = 0, \quad \varphi_1 = \tfrac{1}{4}, \quad \varphi_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad \varphi_3 = \tfrac{3}{4}.
\]

\subsection{The QPE Input State}

We cannot easily prepare $\Ket{u_s}$ for a specific $s$.  Instead, note:
\[
  \frac{1}{2}\sum_{s=0}^3 \Ket{u_s}
  = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{s=0}^3\sum_{k=0}^3 e^{-2\pi\ii sk/4}\Ket{7^k\bmod 15}
  = \Ket{1},
\]
since the sum over $s$ produces a delta function at $k=0$.  So we input
$\Ket{1}$ to the eigenstate register — a state with equal weight $|c_s|^2 = 1/4$
on all four eigenstates.

\subsection{QPE Output and Period Extraction}

With $n = 4$ ancilla qubits (so $N = 16 \geq r^2 = 16$; we need $N \geq r^2$
for the continued-fraction step to succeed reliably), \QPE{} produces the
phase estimate $\tilde\varphi_s \approx s/4$ for each $s$ with equal probability.

Each possible measurement outcome $k \in \{0,1,\ldots,15\}$ gives
$\tilde\varphi = k/16$.  The continued-fraction algorithm applied to $k/16$
finds the best rational approximation with denominator $\leq \sqrt{16}=4$:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}cccc@{}}
\toprule
Measured $k$ & $\tilde\varphi = k/16$ & Best rational approx & Gives period \\
\midrule
0  & $0/16 = 0$   & $0/1$  & trivial ($r$ not found) \\
4  & $4/16 = 1/4$ & $1/4$  & $r = 4$ \\
8  & $8/16 = 1/2$ & $1/2$  & $r = 2$ (divisor of 4) \\
12 & $12/16 = 3/4$& $3/4$  & $r = 4$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

From $r=4$: $\gcd(7^2-1, 15) = \gcd(48, 15) = \gcd(3, 15) = 3$ and
$\gcd(7^2+1, 15) = \gcd(50, 15) = 5$.  So $15 = 3\times 5$. $\square$

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{Why $n \geq 2\log_2 r$?}\ The continued-fraction step needs to
  distinguish $s/r$ from nearby fractions with denominator $< r$.
  By the theory of continued fractions, if $|k/N - s/r| < 1/(2r^2)$,
  then $s/r$ is the unique best rational approximation.
  With $N = 2^n \geq r^2$, the resolution $1/N \leq 1/r^2 < 1/(2r^2)$
  after rounding, guaranteeing the condition is met.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Spectral Leakage and the Dirichlet Kernel}
\label{app:leakage}

\subsection{The QPE Probability as a Dirichlet Kernel}

When $\varphi$ is not exactly representable, the probability distribution
$P(k)$ is not a perfect spike.  The distribution is precisely characterised
by the \emph{Dirichlet kernel}, a classical signal-processing object.

Define the shifted variable $\delta_k = \varphi - k/N$ (where $N=2^n$).
From Appendix~\ref{app:geosum}:
\[
  P(k) = \frac{1}{N^2}\cdot\frac{\sin^2(\pi N \delta_k)}{\sin^2(\pi \delta_k)}
       =: \frac{1}{N^2}\,\mathcal{D}_N(\delta_k),
\]
where $\mathcal{D}_N(\delta) = \frac{\sin^2(\pi N\delta)}{\sin^2(\pi\delta)}$
is the \textbf{squared Dirichlet kernel}.  Key properties:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $\mathcal{D}_N(0) = N^2$ (the exact case: probability 1 at $k=f$).
  \item $\mathcal{D}_N(m/N) = 0$ for any non-zero integer $m$ (perfect
        destructive interference at all other grid points when $\varphi$ is exact).
  \item The ``main lobe'' has width $\approx 2/N$ (the probability is
        concentrated in 2 grid spacings around the peak).
  \item ``Side lobes'' decay as $\sim 1/(\pi N \delta_k)^2$ for large $|\delta_k|$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Why Side Lobes Appear}

The side lobe structure is the quantum analogue of spectral leakage in the
Fast Fourier Transform.  When $\varphi$ is not on the grid, the ``frequency''
does not fit exactly into the $N$-point DFT window, and energy leaks into
neighbouring bins.  The falloff rate $\sim 1/\delta_k^2$ is relatively slow
— this is the same poor windowing as a rectangular window in classical
signal processing.

\subsection{Tail Probability Bound}

How much probability leaks beyond distance $\ell$ from the peak?
\[
  \sum_{|k-b| > \ell} P(k)
  \;\leq\; \sum_{|k-b|>\ell} \frac{1}{(\pi N|\delta_k|)^2}
  \;\approx\; \frac{1}{\pi^2 \ell}.
\]
For $\ell = 1/\epsilon - 1/2$, the tail probability is at most $\epsilon/(\pi^2/4)$.
This is used to derive the precise precision theorem:
to guarantee $|\tilde\varphi - \varphi| \leq 1/2^t$ with probability
$\geq 1-\epsilon$, use $n = t + \lceil\log_2(2+1/(2\epsilon))\rceil$ qubits.

\subsection{Improving the Probability: Amplitude Amplification}

The $40.5\%$ worst-case success probability can be improved without
adding ancilla qubits by using \emph{quantum amplitude amplification}
(a generalisation of Grover's algorithm).  Applying $\lceil\pi/(4\theta)\rceil$
amplification steps (where $\sin^2\theta = p$) boosts $p \to \approx 1$
at a cost of $\bigO(1/\sqrt{p})$ additional oracle calls.  For $p = 4/\pi^2$,
this gives $\bigO(1)$ additional calls.

\section{The Hadamard Test and Its Relation to QPE}
\label{app:hadamard}

\subsection{The Single-Qubit Hadamard Test}

The \textbf{Hadamard test} is the simplest quantum circuit for estimating
an expectation value of a unitary $U$ with respect to a state $\Ket{\psi}$.

\[
\begin{quantikz}[row sep=0.4cm, column sep=0.6cm]
  \lstick{$\Ket{0}$} & \gate{H} & \ctrl{1} & \gate{H} & \meter{} \\
  \lstick{$\Ket{\psi}$} & \qwbundle{m} & \gate{U} & \qw & \qw
\end{quantikz}
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Analysis.}\ The ancilla evolves as:
\begin{align*}
  \Ket{0}\Ket{\psi}
  &\xrightarrow{H\otimes\Id}
    \frac{\Ket{0}+\Ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}\Ket{\psi} \\
  &\xrightarrow{\cU}
    \frac{\Ket{0}\Ket{\psi}+\Ket{1}U\Ket{\psi}}{\sqrt{2}} \\
  &\xrightarrow{H\otimes\Id}
    \frac{(\Ket{0}+\Ket{1})\Ket{\psi}+(\Ket{0}-\Ket{1})U\Ket{\psi}}{2} \\
  &= \Ket{0}\frac{\Ket{\psi}+U\Ket{\psi}}{2}
     + \Ket{1}\frac{\Ket{\psi}-U\Ket{\psi}}{2}.
\end{align*}
The probability of measuring $0$ is:
\[
  P(0)
  = \left\|\frac{\Ket{\psi}+U\Ket{\psi}}{2}\right\|^2
  = \frac{1+\operatorname{Re}\Bra{\psi}U\Ket{\psi}}{2}.
\]
Similarly, $P(0)$ for the circuit with an extra $S^\dagger$ gate before the
final $H$ gives $\frac{1+\operatorname{Im}\Bra{\psi}U\Ket{\psi}}{2}$.

\subsection{Connection to QPE}

The Hadamard test and QPE are deeply related:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Each step of \textbf{iterative QPE} is a Hadamard test with $U^{2^{k-1}}$.
  \item The full \QPE{} circuit can be viewed as $n$ simultaneous Hadamard tests
        (one per ancilla qubit), where the \IQFT{} coherently combines their outputs.
  \item The Hadamard test estimates $\operatorname{Re}\Bra{\psi}U^k\Ket{\psi}$,
        which equals $\operatorname{Re}\,e^{2\pi\ii\varphi k} = \cos(2\pi\varphi k)$
        when $\Ket{\psi}$ is an eigenstate.  The \IQFT{} then inverts the
        discrete Fourier series $\sum_k \cos(2\pi\varphi k)$, recovering $\varphi$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{Operational view.}\ The $n$-qubit \QPE{} circuit simultaneously
  estimates $n$ Fourier coefficients $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi\cdot 2^k}$ for
  $k=0,\ldots,n-1$ via phase kickback.  The \IQFT{} then coherently combines
  these into a single sharp peak at $\varphi$.  This is analogous to how
  the FFT combines $n$ single-frequency measurements into a full spectrum.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Fault-Tolerant QPE and Quantum Resource Estimation}
\label{app:ft}

\subsection{Why Fault Tolerance is Necessary}

Current quantum hardware has gate error rates $\epsilon_g \sim 10^{-3}$.
A QPE circuit for a 50-qubit Hamiltonian (relevant for small molecules in
quantum chemistry) might require $n=20$ ancilla qubits and $\bigO(n\cdot 2^{50})$
controlled-$U$ applications (before squaring) — leading to catastrophic
error accumulation.

\emph{Fault-tolerant quantum computing} (FTQC) uses quantum error-correcting
codes to suppress logical error rates to arbitrarily small values.  The
leading paradigm is the \textbf{surface code}.

\subsection{The Surface Code and Magic State Distillation}

A surface code of \emph{code distance} $d$ encodes 1 logical qubit into
$\bigO(d^2)$ physical qubits and suppresses the logical error rate to
$\epsilon_L \approx (10\,\epsilon_g)^{\lceil d/2\rceil}$.  For $\epsilon_g = 10^{-3}$
and $d=7$: $\epsilon_L \approx 10^{-11}$.

Clifford gates (including $H$, $S$, $\text{CNOT}$) are transversal in the
surface code.  The non-Clifford $T$ gate (our running example!) requires
\emph{magic state distillation}: prepare many noisy $\Ket{T}$ states and
distil one high-quality one via a distillation circuit.  This dominates
the resource cost of fault-tolerant QPE.

\subsection{Logical Qubit and \texorpdfstring{$T$}{T}-Gate Requirements for Quantum Chemistry QPE}

For a molecule with $\eta$ electrons and orbital basis of size $M$:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Resource} & \textbf{Requirement} & \textbf{Notes} \\
\midrule
Logical qubits (data)     & $\bigO(M)$ & Represent molecular orbitals \\
Ancilla qubits (QPE)      & $n \approx 20$--$50$ & Chemical accuracy $\approx 10^{-3}$ Ha \\
$T$-gate count            & $\bigO(M^5/\epsilon)$  & From Trotter simulation of $e^{-\ii Ht}$ \\
Physical qubits (surface code) & $\sim 10^6$--$10^9$ & For large molecules \\
Runtime                   & Hours to days & At MHz physical gate clock \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The $\bigO(M^5)$ $T$-gate count (Babbush et al., 2019~\cite{babbush2019}) can
be reduced to $\bigO(M^{4+o(1)})$ using qubitization techniques (Berry et al., 2019~\cite{berry2019}).

\subsection{Qubitization: A Superior Approach to Hamiltonian Simulation}

Instead of implementing $U = e^{-\ii Ht}$ via Trotter steps, the
\textbf{qubitization} approach directly encodes $H/\|H\|_1$ into a unitary
walk operator $W$ (using a technique called \emph{block encoding}) such that:
\[
  W\Ket{0}\Ket{v}
  = \sin(\theta)\Ket{0}\Ket{H/\|H\|_1 v} + \cos(\theta)\Ket{1}\Ket{\perp},
\]
where the eigenvalues of $H/\|H\|_1$ are in $[-1,1]$ and can be written as
$\cos(\theta)$.  QPE on $W$ extracts $\theta$, from which the energy is
recovered as $E = \|H\|_1 \cos(\theta)$.  This gives a \emph{quadratic
improvement} in the cost of Hamiltonian simulation:
Trotter costs $\bigO(M^5/\epsilon)$ $T$-gates;
qubitization costs $\bigO(M^5/\sqrt{\epsilon})$ or better.

\subsection{Bayesian QPE}

Bayesian QPE treats $\varphi$ as a random variable with a prior distribution
$p_0(\varphi)$.  At each step, the experimenter adaptively chooses the power
$k$ (not necessarily a power of 2!), applies the Hadamard test, and updates
the posterior using Bayes' rule:
\[
  p_{t+1}(\varphi) \propto
  P(\text{outcome}_t \mid \varphi, k_t)\cdot p_t(\varphi).
\]
Because the likelihood function
$P(\text{outcome}=0\mid\varphi,k) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\cos(2\pi\varphi k))$
is smooth, the posterior sharpens rapidly: after $\bigO(n)$ adaptive
experiments the posterior has standard deviation $\sim 2^{-n}$, matching
the standard QPE precision at the same oracle cost.

The advantage: Bayesian QPE is more robust to decoherence, can use
non-power-of-2 queries (allowing optimal experimental design), and provides
uncertainty quantification directly.

\subsection{Robust QPE}

Robust QPE (Kimmel et al., 2015~\cite{kimmel2015}) is designed to work
even when the controlled-$U$ gate has coherent errors.  Instead of assuming
$U$ is applied exactly, it tolerates a worst-case gate error $\epsilon_g$
per layer and still recovers $\varphi$ to precision $1/2^n$ with
high probability, provided $\epsilon_g \lesssim 1/n$.

\section{Quantum Chemistry Case Study: Ground State Energy of \texorpdfstring{H$_2$}{H2}}
\label{app:h2}

\subsection{Physical Setup}

The hydrogen molecule H$_2$ (two protons, two electrons) has a Hamiltonian
$H$ that, in the STO-3G minimal basis (4 spin-orbitals), can be mapped to
a 2-qubit operator via the Jordan--Wigner transform (after accounting for
the frozen core approximation):
\[
  H = h_{00} Z_0 + h_{11} Z_1 + h_{01} Z_0 Z_1 + g_{0011}(X_0 X_1 + Y_0 Y_1),
\]
where $X_k, Y_k, Z_k$ are Pauli operators on qubit $k$, and the coefficients
$h_{ij}$, $g_{0011}$ depend on the internuclear distance $R$.  For $R = 0.735\,\text{\AA}$
(equilibrium bond length):
\[
  h_{00} \approx -0.4488,\quad h_{11} \approx -0.4488,\quad
  h_{01} \approx 0.1810,\quad g_{0011} \approx 0.1810.
\]
(All values in Hartree.)

\subsection{Constructing the Time-Evolution Operator}

The time-evolution operator for time $t$ is $U = e^{-\ii Ht}$.  For QPE we need
controlled-$U^{2^k}$, i.e.\ controlled-$e^{-\ii H t 2^k}$.

Using the first-order Trotter decomposition:
\[
  e^{-\ii Ht} \approx
    e^{-\ii h_{00} Z_0 t}
    \cdot e^{-\ii h_{11} Z_1 t}
    \cdot e^{-\ii h_{01} Z_0 Z_1 t}
    \cdot e^{-\ii g_{0011}(X_0 X_1 + Y_0 Y_1) t}.
\]
Each factor is a standard rotation gate.  For example,
$e^{-\ii\theta Z} = R_Z(2\theta)$ and
$e^{-\ii\theta(XX+YY)} = e^{-\ii\theta XX}\cdot e^{-\ii\theta YY}$,
each decomposable into at most 6 CNOT gates and single-qubit rotations.

\subsection{QPE Circuit for \texorpdfstring{H$_2$}{H2}}

With $n = 8$ ancilla qubits (providing precision $\sim 4\times 10^{-3}$ Ha,
within chemical accuracy), the QPE circuit for H$_2$ requires:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item 8 Hadamard gates (on ancilla).
  \item 8 controlled-$U^{2^k}$ operations for $k=0,\ldots,7$; each
        controlled-$U^{2^k}$ is implemented as $2^k$ Trotter steps, costing
        $\bigO(2^k)$ CNOT gates (without squaring) or $\bigO(k)$ (with squaring).
  \item One 8-qubit \IQFT{} ($\bigO(8^2) = 64$ gates).
  \item 8 measurements.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Expected Measurement Outcome}

The ground-state energy of H$_2$ at equilibrium is $E_0 \approx -1.1373$ Ha.
For time $t = 1$ (atomic units), the corresponding phase is:
\[
  \varphi_0 = \frac{E_0 t}{2\pi} + \frac{1}{2}
  = \frac{-1.1373}{2\pi} + 0.5
  \approx 0.5 - 0.181
  = 0.319,
\]
where the shift by $1/2$ is applied to keep $\varphi_0 \in [0,1)$ when
$E_0 < 0$.  (In practice one calibrates $t$ so that $\varphi_0 \approx 1/4$
for maximum precision.)

With $n=8$ qubits, $\varphi_0 \cdot 256 \approx 81.7$, so the most likely
measurement outcome is $k=82$, giving:
\[
  \tilde\varphi = \frac{82}{256} \approx 0.3203,\quad
  \tilde E_0 = 2\pi(\tilde\varphi - 0.5)\cdot\frac{1}{t}
  \approx 2\pi\cdot(-0.180) \approx -1.131 \text{ Ha}.
\]
The error $|\tilde E_0 - E_0| \approx 0.006$ Ha is just outside chemical
accuracy; using $n=10$ would achieve $\lesssim 10^{-3}$ Ha.

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{Why quantum chemistry is hard classically.}\ The H$_2$ example
  above seems trivial — only 2 qubits for the electrons!  However, for a
  molecule with $\eta$ electrons in $M$ orbitals, the Hamiltonian
  acts on a $\binom{M}{\eta}$-dimensional space.  For iron-sulfur clusters
  (relevant in nitrogen fixation), $M \sim 50$ and $\eta \sim 30$,
  giving Hilbert space dimension $\sim 10^{14}$ — completely infeasible
  classically but tractable with a fault-tolerant quantum computer using
  $\bigO(M)$ logical qubits.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Extensions: Multi-Phase and Continuous-Variable QPE}
\label{app:extensions}

\subsection{Multi-Phase Estimation}

Suppose $U$ has multiple eigenvalues $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi_1}, \ldots, e^{2\pi\ii\varphi_r}$
and we wish to estimate all of them simultaneously.  Running \QPE{} with
eigenstate $\Ket{\chi} = \sum_j c_j\Ket{u_j}$ outputs eigenphase $\varphi_j$
with probability $|c_j|^2$.  To estimate all $r$ phases:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Repeat QPE $\bigO(r \log r / \epsilon^2)$ times and collect
        a histogram of measurement outcomes.
  \item Cluster the outcomes around the $r$ peaks.  Each cluster centroid
        approximates one eigenphase.
\end{itemize}
This requires knowing $r$ a priori or using a clustering algorithm such as
$k$-means on the measurement histogram.

\subsection{Continuous-Variable QPE}

In the continuous-variable (CV) formalism (photonic quantum computing),
qubits are replaced by \emph{modes} with infinite-dimensional Hilbert space.
The CV analogue of QPE estimates the eigenvalue of a unitary acting on a
bosonic mode using \emph{homodyne measurement} instead of computational-basis
measurement.  The \IQFT{} is replaced by a phase-space rotation.  CV-QPE
is used in Gaussian boson sampling and can achieve Heisenberg-limited
phase estimation (standard deviation $\sim 1/N$) with $N$ photons.

\subsection{Heisenberg Limit vs.\ Standard Quantum Limit}

Standard QPE uses $N$ queries to $U$ and achieves precision $\sigma_\varphi \sim 1/N$.
This is the \textbf{Heisenberg limit} — the best possible with $N$ queries.
By contrast, a classical ``frequentist'' scheme using $N$ independent
single-query experiments achieves only $\sigma_\varphi \sim 1/\sqrt{N}$
(the \textbf{standard quantum limit}).  \QPE{} achieves a quadratic improvement
by exploiting quantum coherence between the $N$ queries.

\begin{tcolorbox}[insight]
  \textbf{QPE is Heisenberg-optimal.}\ One can show (via quantum Fisher
  information bounds) that no quantum algorithm using $N$ controlled-$U$
  queries can achieve $\sigma_\varphi < 1/(2\pi N)$.  Standard QPE matches
  this bound (up to constants), making it \emph{optimal} in the query complexity
  sense.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Numerical Simulation: Python Implementation of QPE}
\label{app:code}

The following Python pseudocode (using NumPy and conceptual quantum operations)
implements the 3-qubit QPE for the $T$ gate, reproducing the exact calculation
of Section~\ref{sec:example}.

\begin{verbatim}
import numpy as np

# Define T gate and powers
omega = np.exp(2j * np.pi / 8)      # primitive 8th root of unity
T = np.array([[1, 0], [0, omega]])   # T gate = e^{i*pi/4} on |1>

# QPE state vector simulation (phase register: 3 qubits, 8 dimensions)
N = 8   # 2^3
psi = np.zeros(N, dtype=complex)

# After H^(x3): uniform superposition
psi = np.ones(N, dtype=complex) / np.sqrt(N)

# After controlled-T^{2^k} gates: multiply basis state |j> by e^{2pi*i*phi*j}
phi = 1/8
for j in range(N):
    psi[j] *= np.exp(2j * np.pi * phi * j)

# After inverse QFT: apply N-point inverse DFT
psi = np.fft.ifft(psi) * np.sqrt(N)  # ifft includes 1/N normalisation

# Measurement probabilities
probs = np.abs(psi)**2
print("Probabilities:", np.round(probs, 6))
# Expected output: [0, 1, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0]
# i.e., P(k=1) = 1, all others 0.

# Read off phase
k_measured = np.argmax(probs)
phi_est = k_measured / N
print(f"Estimated phase: {phi_est}")   # 0.125 = 1/8
\end{verbatim}

Note that the NumPy \texttt{ifft} applies the normalisation $1/N$ while
the quantum \IQFT{} applies $1/\sqrt{N}$.  The factor \texttt{np.sqrt(N)}
corrects for this.

For a non-exact phase such as $\varphi = 1/3$, replace \texttt{phi = 1/8}
with \texttt{phi = 1/3} and observe a peaked but spread probability distribution,
matching the analysis of Section~\ref{sec:nonexact}.

\section{Historical Notes: The Development of QPE}
\label{app:history}

\textbf{1993 — Bernstein and Vazirani.}\ The first quantum algorithm to exploit
interference for phase estimation was implicit in the Bernstein--Vazirani
algorithm, which extracts a hidden bit-string from a linear function.

\textbf{1994 — Shor.}\ Peter Shor's factoring algorithm~\cite{shor1994} was the
first explicit use of what we now call \QPE{}.  He used the quantum FFT
(discovered independently around the same time) to extract the period $r$
from the eigenvalues of the modular exponentiation unitary.  This paper single-handedly launched the field of quantum algorithms.

\textbf{1995 — Kitaev.}\ Alexei Kitaev formalised the \QPE{} primitive in
full generality~\cite{kitaev1995}, introducing the abelian stabilizer problem
and the iterative variant (IQPE) using a single ancilla qubit.  He also proved
the precision and complexity guarantees that are now standard.

\textbf{1996 — Abrams and Lloyd.}\ Abrams and Lloyd proposed applying QPE
to the time-evolution operator $e^{-\ii Ht}$ to compute eigenvalues of
quantum Hamiltonians~\cite{abrams1997}, founding quantum simulation as a field.

\textbf{1999 — Mosca and Ekert.}\ Mosca and Ekert gave a clean pedagogical
treatment of QPE in the context of the hidden subgroup problem, clarifying
the connection to the QFT.

\textbf{2005 — Aspuru-Guzik et al.}\ The first concrete proposal to use
QPE for quantum chemistry~\cite{aspuru2005}, estimating molecular ground-state
energies — the application that continues to drive quantum hardware development.

\textbf{2009 — HHL algorithm.}\ Harrow, Hassidim, and Lloyd used QPE
as a subroutine in the first exponentially-fast quantum linear-systems
algorithm~\cite{harrow2009}.

\textbf{2018--present — Qubitization and block encoding.}\ Berry, Babbush,
and collaborators developed qubitization~\cite{babbush2019,berry2019},
reducing the $T$-gate cost of QPE for chemistry from $\bigO(M^5/\epsilon)$
to $\bigO(M^{4+o(1)}/\sqrt{\epsilon})$, making fault-tolerant QPE for
100-electron molecules plausible with $\sim 10^6$ physical qubits.

\section{Glossary of Key Terms}
\label{app:glossary}

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=3cm, font=\bfseries\color{qblue}]
  \item[Ancilla qubit]
    An auxiliary qubit used as a control or workspace.  In \QPE{}, the $n$
    ancilla qubits form the phase register.

  \item[Binary fraction]
    A number of the form $0.b_1 b_2\cdots b_n = \sum_{k=1}^n b_k 2^{-k}$.
    The output of \QPE{} is a binary fraction approximating $\varphi$.

  \item[Block encoding]
    A technique that embeds a non-unitary matrix $A$ as the top-left block
    of a larger unitary: $U = \begin{pmatrix}A/\|A\| & \cdots \\ \cdots & \cdots\end{pmatrix}$.
    Used in qubitization-based QPE.

  \item[Chemical accuracy]
    An error threshold of $\approx 1$ kcal/mol $\approx 1.6\times10^{-3}$ Ha
    in energy calculations.  This is the target precision for quantum chemistry applications of \QPE{}.

  \item[Controlled-$U$ gate]
    A gate that applies $U$ to the target register if and only if the control qubit is $\Ket{1}$.

  \item[Dirichlet kernel]
    The function $\mathcal{D}_N(\delta) = \sin^2(\pi N\delta)/\sin^2(\pi\delta)$
    that characterises the \QPE{} probability distribution.

  \item[Eigenphase]
    The phase $\varphi \in [0,1)$ such that the eigenvalue of the unitary $U$
    is $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi}$.

  \item[Eigenstate]
    A state $\Ket{\psi}$ satisfying $U\Ket{\psi} = \lambda\Ket{\psi}$ for some scalar $\lambda$.

  \item[Heisenberg limit]
    The optimal phase estimation precision of $\sigma_\varphi \sim 1/N$ achievable with $N$ queries.

  \item[Inverse QFT (IQFT)]
    The adjoint of the Quantum Fourier Transform; the decoder step of \QPE{}.

  \item[Magic state distillation]
    A fault-tolerant procedure for producing high-fidelity $T$-gate resource states from many noisy copies.

  \item[Phase kickback]
    The mechanism by which the phase $e^{2\pi\ii\varphi}$ migrates from the eigenstate register to the control qubit when the eigenstate undergoes a controlled unitary.

  \item[Phase register]
    The $n$-qubit ancilla register in \QPE{} that stores the estimate of $\varphi$.

  \item[Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT)]
    The unitary $\QFT\Ket{j} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_k e^{2\pi\ii jk/N}\Ket{k}$,
    the quantum analogue of the discrete Fourier transform.

  \item[Qubitization]
    A technique for Hamiltonian simulation that achieves near-optimal query complexity for QPE in quantum chemistry.

  \item[Resolution]
    The smallest difference $1/2^n$ between representable phase values
    with $n$ ancilla qubits.

  \item[Spectral leakage]
    Probability appearing at measurement outcomes far from the true phase,
    analogous to spectral leakage in classical FFT with finite windows.

  \item[Surface code]
    A topological quantum error-correcting code with threshold $\sim 1\%$,
    the leading candidate for fault-tolerant quantum computation.

  \item[Trotter decomposition]
    An approximation $e^{-\ii(A+B)t} \approx e^{-\ii At}\cdot e^{-\ii Bt} + \bigO(t^2)$
    used to simulate Hamiltonian time evolution on a quantum computer.

  \item[Unitary operator]
    A linear operator $U$ satisfying $U^\dagger U = \Id$.  All eigenvalues lie on the complex unit circle.
\end{description}
