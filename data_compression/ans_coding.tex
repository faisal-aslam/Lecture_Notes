\section{Asymmetric Numeral Systems (ANS)}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Motivation and Historical Context}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

Before diving into ANS, it helps to understand \emph{why} it was invented.
Entropy coders aim to assign codes whose lengths approach the theoretical
entropy limit.  Two classical approaches dominated for decades:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Huffman Coding} --- assigns integer-length codewords; fast
          but wastes bits when symbol probabilities are not powers of
          $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
    \item \textbf{Arithmetic Coding} --- can approach the entropy limit
          arbitrarily closely, but requires divisions and multi-precision
          arithmetic, making it comparatively slow.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
In 2009, Jarek Duda introduced \textbf{Asymmetric Numeral Systems (ANS)}, a
family of entropy coders that achieves
\emph{arithmetic-coding--level compression efficiency} while running at
speeds comparable to Huffman coding.
ANS powers many modern compression tools, including
\textbf{Zstandard (zstd)}, \textbf{LZ4 entropy back-end},
\textbf{LZFSE} (Apple), and \textbf{Facebook's Brotli extensions}.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Core Intuition: Numbers as a Stream of Information}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

The key insight of ANS is to treat the \textbf{state} $x$ --- a single
natural number --- as a compressed bit-stream.

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{State.}  In ANS the encoder maintains a single integer
$x \in \mathbb{N}$.  Encoding a symbol \emph{updates} $x$; decoding a
symbol \emph{recovers} the original $x$.  The final value of $x$, written
out in binary, is the compressed output.
\end{definitionbox}

Think of $x$ as a bucket that carries all previously encoded information.
Each new symbol pours more information into the bucket according to its
probability; rare symbols pour in more bits than common ones --- exactly
mirroring the entropy formula $-\log_2 p$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The ANS Alphabet and Probability Model}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Setup.}  Let the source alphabet be
$\mathcal{A} = \{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_k\}$ with probabilities
$\{p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_k\}$, $\sum_i p_i = 1$.

ANS works with \emph{quantised} probabilities.  Choose a table size
$L = 2^R$ (a power of~2).  Each symbol $s_i$ is assigned a frequency
$f_i$ (a positive integer) such that $\sum_i f_i = L$.  The quantised
probability is $\tilde{p}_i = f_i / L$.
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{importantbox}
\textbf{Why a power-of-two table size?}  With $L = 2^R$, normalisation and
range checks reduce to fast bit-shift and bit-mask operations
($x \bmod L = x\ \&\ (L{-}1)$), making ANS extremely cache-friendly and
branch-free on modern CPUs.
\end{importantbox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Two Flavours of ANS}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

The ANS family has two widely used variants:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Full Name} & \textbf{Used in} \\
\midrule
\textbf{tANS} & Tabled ANS & Zstandard, LZFSE \\
\textbf{rANS} & Range ANS  & Zstandard, CRAM (bioinformatics) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Both share the same mathematical core; they differ in how the coding tables
are stored and accessed.  We study \textbf{rANS} first because its arithmetic
is transparent, then show how \textbf{tANS} replaces the arithmetic with a
pre-built lookup table.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{rANS --- Range Asymmetric Numeral Systems}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{The State Range}

The encoder keeps state $x$ in the \emph{normalised range} $[L, 2L)$ where
$L = 2^R$.  After encoding or decoding each symbol, $x$ is renormalised back
into $[L, 2L)$ by streaming bits in or out.

\subsubsection{Encoding a Symbol}

The encoding formula maps a state $x$ and symbol $s$ to a new state $x'$:
\[
  x' \;=\; \left\lfloor \frac{x}{f_s} \right\rfloor \cdot L
         \;+\; c_s \;+\; (x \bmod f_s)
\]
For the decoder to correctly identify the symbol from $x' \bmod L$, we need
$x' \bmod L \in [c_s, c_s + f_s)$.  Inspecting the formula:
\[
  x' \bmod L \;=\; c_s + (x \bmod f_s)
\]
This lands in $[c_s, c_s + f_s)$ for any $x$ --- the slot tag is always
correct.  However, the formula may produce $x'$ outside $[L, 2L)$, and
\emph{renormalisation must not disturb} $x' \bmod L$.  Right-shifting
$x'$ to shrink it would scramble the remainder, so we instead
\textbf{pre-normalise}: emit bits from $x$ \emph{before} applying the
formula, bringing $x$ into $[f_s, 2f_s)$.  With $x \in [f_s, 2f_s)$ we
have $\lfloor x/f_s \rfloor = 1$, so:
\[
  x' = L + c_s + (x \bmod f_s)
  \;\in\; [L + c_s,\; L + c_s + f_s) \;\subset\; [L, 2L)
\]
The output lands in $[L, 2L)$ with no further adjustment needed.

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{rANS Encode.}  To encode symbol $s$ with frequency $f_s$ and
cumulative frequency $c_s = \sum_{i < s} f_i$:

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x \ge 2f_s$}
    \State $\text{emit}(x \mathrel{\&} 1)$ \Comment{output LSB of $x$}
    \State $x \leftarrow x \gg 1$          \Comment{divide by 2 (floor)}
\EndWhile
\Comment{Now $f_s \le x < 2f_s$; apply formula:}
\State $x \leftarrow \lfloor x / f_s \rfloor \cdot L + c_s + (x \bmod f_s)$
\Comment{Result is guaranteed to be in $[L, 2L)$}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{importantbox}
\textbf{Streaming direction.}  The \emph{encoder} outputs bits
\emph{before} applying the encoding formula (pre-normalisation), and the
\emph{decoder} reads those bits \emph{after} inverting the formula
(post-normalisation upward), in \textbf{reverse order}.  This means the
decoder recovers symbols in reverse: encode $s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n$;
decode $s_n, \ldots, s_2, s_1$.  Practical implementations therefore
encode the block \emph{backwards} so the decoded order is automatically
correct.
\end{importantbox}

\subsubsection{Decoding a Symbol}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{rANS Decode.}  Given state $x \in [L, 2L)$, recover symbol $s$
and the previous encoder state $x_{\text{prev}}$:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item Compute $r = x \bmod L$.  Find symbol $s$ such that
          $c_s \leq r < c_s + f_s$. \hfill (slot lookup)
    \item Compute $x_{\text{prev}} = f_s \cdot \lfloor x / L \rfloor
          + r - c_s$. \hfill (inverse formula)
    \item Renormalise $x_{\text{prev}}$ upward into $[L, 2L)$:
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x_{\text{prev}} < L$}
    \State Read next bit $b$ from the stream
    \State $x_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow (x_{\text{prev}} \ll 1) \mid b$
           \Comment{shift left, insert bit}
\EndWhile
\Comment{Now $L \le x_{\text{prev}} < 2L$}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{enumerate}
\end{definitionbox}

Steps 1--2 are the \emph{exact algebraic inverses} of the encode formula.
Since $x \in [L, 2L)$, we have $\lfloor x/L \rfloor = 1$, so
$x_{\text{prev}} = f_s + r - c_s \in [f_s, 2f_s)$ --- precisely the
pre-normalised range the encoder started from.  Renormalising upward then
consumes the bits the encoder emitted, restoring the encoder's state before
it encoded $s$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Worked Example: rANS Encoding}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Setup.}  Alphabet $\{A, B, C\}$, table size $L = 8$ ($R = 3$ bits).

\medskip
\begin{tabular}{@{}lllll@{}}
\toprule
Symbol & Freq.\ $f$ & Cumul.\ $c$ & Prob. & Slot range $[c,\;c\!+\!f)$ \\
\midrule
$A$ & 4 & 0 & $0.50$ & $[0,\,4)$ \\
$B$ & 2 & 4 & $0.25$ & $[4,\,6)$ \\
$C$ & 2 & 6 & $0.25$ & $[6,\,8)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\smallskip
\textit{Reading the table:} Each symbol owns $f$ consecutive slots within
$[0, L)$.  The slot range $[c, c+f)$ is how the decoder identifies which
symbol is present --- it checks which range contains $x \bmod L$.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Goal:} Encode the sequence \texttt{A B A} starting from
initial state $x = L = 8$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The encoding procedure:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Pre-normalise:} emit LSBs of $x$ until $x \in [f_s, 2f_s)$.
  \item \textbf{Apply formula:} $x \leftarrow \lfloor x/f_s \rfloor \cdot L
        + c_s + (x \bmod f_s)$.  With $x \in [f_s, 2f_s)$,
        $\lfloor x/f_s \rfloor = 1$, so $x \leftarrow L + c_s + (x \bmod f_s)
        \in [L, 2L)$ directly.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip\hrule\medskip

% ---- Step 1 ----
\noindent\textbf{Step 1 --- Encode \texttt{A}}\quad
$f_A = 4,\; c_A = 0,\;$ state $x = 8$.

\textit{Pre-normalise into $[f_A, 2f_A) = [4, 8)$:}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x \ge 2f_A = 8$}
    \State $8 \ge 8$:\enspace $\text{emit}(8 \mathrel{\&} 1) = \mathbf{0}$;\enspace
           $x \leftarrow 8 \gg 1 = 4$
\EndWhile
\Comment{$4 \in [4, 8)$: stop}
\end{algorithmic}

\textit{Apply formula} (with pre-normed $x = 4$):
\[
  x \leftarrow \lfloor 4/4 \rfloor \times 8 + 0 + (4 \bmod 4)
     = 1 \times 8 + 0 + 0 = 8
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
  Bit emitted this step: & \texttt{0} \\
  Bitstream so far:      & \texttt{0} \\
  New state $x$:         & $8$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip\hrule\medskip

% ---- Step 2 ----
\noindent\textbf{Step 2 --- Encode \texttt{B}}\quad
$f_B = 2,\; c_B = 4,\;$ state $x = 8$.

\textit{Pre-normalise into $[f_B, 2f_B) = [2, 4)$:}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x \ge 2f_B = 4$}
    \State $8 \ge 4$:\enspace $\text{emit}(8 \mathrel{\&} 1) = \mathbf{0}$;\enspace
           $x \leftarrow 8 \gg 1 = 4$
    \State $4 \ge 4$:\enspace $\text{emit}(4 \mathrel{\&} 1) = \mathbf{0}$;\enspace
           $x \leftarrow 4 \gg 1 = 2$
\EndWhile
\Comment{$2 \in [2, 4)$: stop}
\end{algorithmic}

\textit{Apply formula} (with pre-normed $x = 2$):
\[
  x \leftarrow \lfloor 2/2 \rfloor \times 8 + 4 + (2 \bmod 2)
     = 1 \times 8 + 4 + 0 = 12
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
  Bits emitted this step: & \texttt{0\;0} \\
  Bitstream so far:       & \texttt{0\;0\;0} \\
  New state $x$:          & $12$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\smallskip
\textit{Why two bits?}  $B$ has probability $0.25$, so $x$ must be
shrunk into the range $[2, 4)$ before the formula --- requiring two
right-shifts from $x = 8$.  This is consistent with $B$ carrying
$-\log_2 0.25 = 2$ bits of information.

\medskip\hrule\medskip

% ---- Step 3 ----
\noindent\textbf{Step 3 --- Encode \texttt{A}}\quad
$f_A = 4,\; c_A = 0,\;$ state $x = 12$.

\textit{Pre-normalise into $[f_A, 2f_A) = [4, 8)$:}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x \ge 2f_A = 8$}
    \State $12 \ge 8$:\enspace $\text{emit}(12 \mathrel{\&} 1) = \mathbf{0}$;\enspace
           $x \leftarrow 12 \gg 1 = 6$
\EndWhile
\Comment{$6 \in [4, 8)$: stop}
\end{algorithmic}

\textit{Apply formula} (with pre-normed $x = 6$):
\[
  x \leftarrow \lfloor 6/4 \rfloor \times 8 + 0 + (6 \bmod 4)
     = 1 \times 8 + 0 + 2 = 10
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
  Bit emitted this step: & \texttt{0} \\
  Bitstream so far:      & \texttt{0\;0\;0\;0} \\
  New state $x$:         & $10$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip\hrule\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Finalise.}  Append the final state
$x = 10 = (1010)_2$ to the bitstream.

\[
\underbrace{\mathtt{0\;0\;0\;0}}_{\substack{\text{pre-norm bits} \\
  \text{emitted during steps 1--3}}}
\;\Big|\;
\underbrace{\mathtt{1\;0\;1\;0}}_{\substack{\text{final state} \\ x = 10}}
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step summary:}

\smallskip
\begin{tabular}{@{}clcccll@{}}
\toprule
Step & Sym & $x$ in & Bits emitted & $x$ pre-norm & Formula result & $x$ out \\
\midrule
1 & $A$ & 8  & \texttt{0}    & 4 & $1{\times}8+0+0=8$   & 8  \\
2 & $B$ & 8  & \texttt{0\;0} & 2 & $1{\times}8+4+0=12$  & 12 \\
3 & $A$ & 12 & \texttt{0}    & 6 & $1{\times}8+0+2=10$  & 10 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip
\textit{Entropy check.}
The information content of \texttt{A B A} is
$-(2\log_2 0.5 + \log_2 0.25) = 1 + 1 + 2 = 4$ bits.
We emitted exactly 4 pre-norm bits $+$ 4 bits for the final state
$= 8$ bits total.  The 4-bit overhead is a fixed per-block cost that
becomes negligible for longer sequences.
\end{examplebox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Worked Example: rANS Decoding}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Goal.}  Decode the bitstream produced above and verify that we
recover exactly \texttt{A B A} --- the same sequence that was encoded.

\medskip
\begin{tabular}{@{}lllll@{}}
\toprule
Symbol & Freq.\ $f$ & Cumul.\ $c$ & Prob. & Slot range $[c,\;c\!+\!f)$ \\
\midrule
$A$ & 4 & 0 & $0.50$ & $[0,\,4)$ \\
$B$ & 2 & 4 & $0.25$ & $[4,\,6)$ \\
$C$ & 2 & 6 & $0.25$ & $[6,\,8)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip
Working range $[L, 2L) = [8, 16)$.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Initialise the decoder.}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Read the stored final state: $(1010)_2 = 10$.  Set $x = 10$.
  \item The encoder emitted pre-norm bits in order \texttt{0, 0, 0, 0}.
        The decoder reads them in \textbf{reverse order}: \texttt{0, 0, 0, 0}
        (identical here since all bits are zero; in general the reversal
        matters and is what allows the decoder to undo each encoding step
        exactly).
\end{itemize}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
  Initial state:          & $x = 10$ \\
  Bit queue (read order): & \texttt{0},\ \texttt{0},\ \texttt{0},\ \texttt{0} \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Decoding formula --- the exact inverse of encoding:}
\[
  \boxed{x_{\text{prev}} \;=\;
    f_s \times \lfloor x / L \rfloor \;+\; (x \bmod L) \;-\; c_s}
\]
Since $x \in [L, 2L)$, $\lfloor x/L \rfloor = 1$, so
$x_{\text{prev}} = f_s + (x \bmod L) - c_s \in [f_s, 2f_s)$.
Renormalising upward then restores $x_{\text{prev}}$ to $[L, 2L)$,
consuming the same bits the encoder emitted for that symbol.

\noindent\textbf{Each decode step:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item Compute $r = x \bmod L$.  Find symbol $s$ with
        $c_s \leq r < c_s + f_s$.
  \item Recover $x_{\text{prev}} = f_s \cdot \lfloor x/L \rfloor + r - c_s$.
  \item Renormalise upward:
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x_{\text{prev}} < L$}
    \State Read next bit $b$ from the queue
    \State $x_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow (x_{\text{prev}} \ll 1) \mid b$
\EndWhile
\Comment{Now $L \le x_{\text{prev}} < 2L$}
\end{algorithmic}
  \item Set $x \leftarrow x_{\text{prev}}$ and repeat.
\end{enumerate}

Because the encoder processed \texttt{A B A} left-to-right, the decoder
undoes the last encoding step first (recovering the final \texttt{A}),
then \texttt{B}, then the first \texttt{A} --- giving the original
sequence in correct order.

\medskip\hrule\medskip

% ---- Decode step 1 ----
\noindent\textbf{Decode Step 1 --- recovers \texttt{A} (position 3, last encoded)}

\medskip
$r = x \bmod L = 10 \bmod 8 = 2$.

\textit{Slot lookup:} $r = 2$ falls in $[0, 4)$, so $s = A$.

\textit{Recover previous state:}
\[
  x_{\text{prev}} = 4 \times \lfloor 10/8 \rfloor + 2 - 0
                  = 4 \times 1 + 2 = 6
\]

\textit{Renormalise upward:}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x_{\text{prev}} < L = 8$}
    \State $6 < 8$:\enspace read bit $\mathbf{0}$;\enspace
           $x_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow (6 \ll 1)\mid 0 = 12$
\EndWhile
\Comment{$12 \ge 8$: stop}
\end{algorithmic}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
  Decoded symbol:         & $A$ \\
  New state $x$:          & $12$ \\
  Bit queue remaining:    & \texttt{0},\ \texttt{0},\ \texttt{0} \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip\hrule\medskip

% ---- Decode step 2 ----
\noindent\textbf{Decode Step 2 --- recovers \texttt{B} (position 2)}

\medskip
$r = x \bmod L = 12 \bmod 8 = 4$.

\textit{Slot lookup:} $r = 4$ falls in $[4, 6)$, so $s = B$.

\textit{Recover previous state:}
\[
  x_{\text{prev}} = 2 \times \lfloor 12/8 \rfloor + 4 - 4
                  = 2 \times 1 + 0 = 2
\]

\textit{Renormalise upward:}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x_{\text{prev}} < L = 8$}
    \State $2 < 8$:\enspace read bit $\mathbf{0}$;\enspace
           $x_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow (2 \ll 1)\mid 0 = 4$
    \State $4 < 8$:\enspace read bit $\mathbf{0}$;\enspace
           $x_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow (4 \ll 1)\mid 0 = 8$
\EndWhile
\Comment{$8 \ge 8$: stop}
\end{algorithmic}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
  Decoded symbol:         & $B$ \\
  New state $x$:          & $8$ \\
  Bit queue remaining:    & \texttt{0} \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip\hrule\medskip

% ---- Decode step 3 ----
\noindent\textbf{Decode Step 3 --- recovers \texttt{A} (position 1, first encoded)}

\medskip
$r = x \bmod L = 8 \bmod 8 = 0$.

\textit{Slot lookup:} $r = 0$ falls in $[0, 4)$, so $s = A$.

\textit{Recover previous state:}
\[
  x_{\text{prev}} = 4 \times \lfloor 8/8 \rfloor + 0 - 0
                  = 4 \times 1 + 0 = 4
\]

\textit{Renormalise upward:}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While{$x_{\text{prev}} < L = 8$}
    \State $4 < 8$:\enspace read bit $\mathbf{0}$;\enspace
           $x_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow (4 \ll 1)\mid 0 = 8$
\EndWhile
\Comment{$8 \ge 8$: stop}
\end{algorithmic}

The bit queue is now empty and all 3 symbols have been decoded.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
  Decoded symbol:      & $A$ \\
  New state $x$:       & $8 = L$ (encoder's initial state) \\
  Bit queue remaining: & (empty) \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip\hrule\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Complete decode summary}

\smallskip
\begin{tabular}{@{}clclcll@{}}
\toprule
Step & $x$ in & $r = x \bmod 8$ & Symbol & $x_{\text{prev}}$ (raw) &
  Bits read & $x$ out \\
\midrule
1 & 10 & 2 & $A$ & 6 & \texttt{0}    & 12 \\
2 & 12 & 4 & $B$ & 2 & \texttt{0\;0} & 8  \\
3 & 8  & 0 & $A$ & 4 & \texttt{0}    & 8  \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip
\textbf{Decoded sequence: \texttt{A B A} --- identical to the encoded input.}

\medskip
\textit{Bit accounting.}  The encoder emitted 4 pre-norm bits; the
decoder consumed all 4 --- one for each \texttt{A} ($-\log_2 0.5 = 1$
bit) and two for \texttt{B} ($-\log_2 0.25 = 2$ bits).  No bits are
left over.  Encode and decode are exact inverses.
\end{examplebox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Understanding the Encoding Formula Intuitively}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

Why does $x \leftarrow \lfloor x/f_s \rfloor \cdot L + c_s + (x \bmod f_s)$ work?

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item $\lfloor x / f_s \rfloor \cdot L$ \textbf{scales} $x$ up by
          $L / f_s$, adding $\log_2(L/f_s) = -\log_2 p_s$ bits to the
          state --- exactly the information content of symbol $s$.  After
          pre-normalisation, $\lfloor x / f_s \rfloor = 1$, so this term
          simply equals $L$.
    \item $c_s + (x \bmod f_s)$ \textbf{tags} the result: since
          $0 \leq x \bmod f_s < f_s$, we have
          $c_s \leq x \bmod L < c_s + f_s$ after the update, so the
          decoder can recover $s$ by checking which slot range contains
          $x \bmod L$.
\end{itemize}

This is the elegance of ANS: encoding a symbol shifts exactly $-\log_2 p_s$
bits of information into $x$, and decoding shifts them back out.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{tANS --- Tabled ANS}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Motivation}

rANS requires one integer division per symbol.  A \emph{division-free}
variant is possible by precomputing everything into a lookup table.  This is
\textbf{tANS}.

\subsubsection{The Symbol-Spread Function}
\label{sec:ans_spread}

The first step is to \textbf{spread} the $L$ table slots among symbols
proportionally to their frequencies.

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Symbol Spread.}  Assign each slot $q \in \{0, 1, \ldots, L{-}1\}$
a symbol $\texttt{sym}[q]$ such that exactly $f_s$ slots are assigned to
symbol $s$.  The strategy used in Zstandard distributes copies of $s$ as
evenly as possible:
\[
  \text{slot for the } i\text{-th copy of } s
  = \left( c_s + \left\lfloor \frac{i \cdot L}{f_s} \right\rfloor \right)
  \bmod L, \qquad i = 0, 1, \ldots, f_s - 1.
\]
\end{definitionbox}

\subsubsection{Building the Encode and Decode Tables}

Once \texttt{sym[]} is fixed, build two tables.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Decode table} \texttt{dec[q]} --- for each slot $q$ stores
          the symbol $\texttt{sym}[q]$ and the pre-renormalised previous
          state
          \[
            x_{\text{prev}}(q) = f_s \cdot \left\lfloor \frac{L+q}{L}
            \right\rfloor + \bigl((L+q) \bmod f_s\bigr) - c_s,
            \quad s = \texttt{sym}[q].
          \]
          (This equals $f_s \cdot 1 + (q \bmod f_s) - c_s$ since
          $\lfloor (L+q)/L \rfloor = 1$ for $0 \leq q < L$.)
    \item \textbf{Encode table} \texttt{enc[s][x mod $f_s$]} --- for each
          symbol $s$ and current state $x$, stores the new state and
          the number of bits to output.
\end{itemize}

At runtime the encode/decode step becomes a single table lookup plus a
handful of bit operations --- no division at all.

\subsubsection{Simple tANS Example}

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Setup.} $L = 8$, symbols $\{A, B\}$,
$f_A = 6,\; c_A = 0$,\quad $f_B = 2,\; c_B = 6$.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 1: Spread symbols into 8 slots.}

Using the even-spacing formula above:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lllllllll@{}}
\toprule
Slot $q$        & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 \\
\midrule
\texttt{sym[q]} & A & A & B & A & A & B & A & A \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

$A$ occupies 6 slots (evenly spaced); $B$ occupies slots 2 and 5.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 2: Build the decode table.}

For each slot $q$, compute $s = \texttt{sym}[q]$ and
$x_{\text{prev}} = f_s \cdot 1 + (q \bmod f_s) - c_s$:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccc@{}}
\toprule
$q$ & $s$ & $f_s$ & $c_s$ & $x_{\text{prev}}(q)$ \\
\midrule
0 & $A$ & 6 & 0 & $6 + (0 \bmod 6) - 0 = 6$ \\
1 & $A$ & 6 & 0 & $6 + (1 \bmod 6) - 0 = 7$ \\
2 & $B$ & 2 & 6 & $2 + (2 \bmod 2) - 6 = -4$\quad$\to$ renorm needed \\
3 & $A$ & 6 & 0 & $6 + (3 \bmod 6) - 0 = 9$\quad$\to$ renorm needed \\
4 & $A$ & 6 & 0 & $6 + (4 \bmod 6) - 0 = 10$\quad$\to$ renorm needed \\
5 & $B$ & 2 & 6 & $2 + (5 \bmod 2) - 6 = -3$\quad$\to$ renorm needed \\
6 & $A$ & 6 & 0 & $6 + (6 \bmod 6) - 0 = 6$ \\
7 & $A$ & 6 & 0 & $6 + (7 \bmod 6) - 0 = 7$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Entries that fall below $L = 8$ after computation have bits read in
to renormalise upward before becoming the working state.  In a real
implementation the table stores the \emph{post-renorm} state directly,
so lookup + shift is a single cheap operation.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 3: Encode with the table.}

Starting from any state $x \in [8, 16)$, to encode symbol $A$ the
encoder looks up \texttt{enc[$A$][$x \bmod f_A$]} which returns the
pre-computed new state and how many bits to output.  No division
is performed at runtime.
\end{examplebox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Why ANS Achieves Near-Entropy Compression}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{theorem}
Let $p_s$ be the true probability of symbol $s$ and
$\tilde{p}_s = f_s / L$ the quantised probability.  The redundancy
(extra bits per symbol) of ANS over the Shannon entropy limit is
bounded by:
\[
  \Delta H \;\leq\; \sum_s p_s \log_2 \frac{p_s}{\tilde{p}_s}
  \;+\; \frac{1}{L \ln 2}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item The first term is the KL divergence between the true and
          quantised distributions --- it vanishes as $L \to \infty$.
    \item The second term $1/(L\ln 2)$ is negligible even for moderate
          $L$: with $L = 4096$ it contributes less than
          $0.0004$ bits/symbol.
    \item In practice, $L = 2^{11}$ or $2^{12}$ gives compression within
          $0.001$ bits/symbol of entropy --- far tighter than Huffman.
\end{itemize}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Interleaved ANS for Parallelism}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

A powerful feature of ANS is that multiple independent state variables
can encode/decode simultaneously --- a technique called
\textbf{interleaved ANS}.

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Interleaved rANS with 2 streams.}

Maintain two states $x_0, x_1$.  Encode even-indexed symbols into $x_0$
and odd-indexed symbols into $x_1$.  The two streams are written and read
independently and can exploit SIMD (Single Instruction Multiple Data)
CPU instructions.

Zstandard uses \textbf{4-way interleaving} for its ANS back-end,
achieving throughput of several gigabytes per second on modern hardware.
\end{examplebox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{ANS in Practice: Zstandard}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

Zstandard (zstd), developed at Meta and standardised as RFC~8878, uses
ANS as its entropy coder:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Literals} --- compressed with Huffman or FSE (Finite
          State Entropy, Meta's name for tANS).
    \item \textbf{Offsets, match lengths, literal lengths} --- compressed
          with FSE tables.
    \item Table size $L = 2^6$ to $2^9$, chosen adaptively per block.
    \item At level 3 (default), zstd decompresses roughly $5\times$ faster
          than gzip while achieving similar or better compression ratios.
\end{itemize}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Step-by-Step: Building a tANS Table from Scratch}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Full tANS table construction for a 3-symbol alphabet.}

\medskip
\textbf{Given:} $\mathcal{A} = \{A, B, C\}$, $L = 8$,
$f_A = 4,\; c_A = 0$;\quad $f_B = 2,\; c_B = 4$;\quad
$f_C = 2,\; c_C = 6$.

\medskip
\textbf{1. Spread symbols.}
Place each symbol's copies evenly across the 8 slots using the
formula from Section~\ref{sec:ans_spread}:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccccccc@{}}
\toprule
Slot $q$        & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 \\
\midrule
\texttt{sym[q]} & A & C & B & A & C & B & A & A \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

$A$ at slots $\{0,3,6,7\}$;\quad $B$ at $\{2,5\}$;\quad
$C$ at $\{1,4\}$.

\medskip
\textbf{2. Build the decode table.}

For each slot $q$, apply
$x_{\text{prev}}(q) = f_s + (q \bmod f_s) - c_s$
(since $\lfloor(L+q)/L\rfloor = 1$ for all $0 \leq q < L = 8$):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}cccccc@{}}
\toprule
$q$ & \texttt{sym[q]} & $f_s$ & $c_s$ & Computation & $x_{\text{prev}}$ \\
\midrule
0 & $A$ & 4 & 0 & $4 + (0 \bmod 4) - 0$ & $4$ \\
1 & $C$ & 2 & 6 & $2 + (1 \bmod 2) - 6$ & $-3$ (renorm $\uparrow$) \\
2 & $B$ & 2 & 4 & $2 + (2 \bmod 2) - 4$ & $-2$ (renorm $\uparrow$) \\
3 & $A$ & 4 & 0 & $4 + (3 \bmod 4) - 0$ & $7$ \\
4 & $C$ & 2 & 6 & $2 + (4 \bmod 2) - 6$ & $-4$ (renorm $\uparrow$) \\
5 & $B$ & 2 & 4 & $2 + (5 \bmod 2) - 4$ & $-1$ (renorm $\uparrow$) \\
6 & $A$ & 4 & 0 & $4 + (6 \bmod 4) - 0$ & $6$ \\
7 & $A$ & 4 & 0 & $4 + (7 \bmod 4) - 0$ & $7$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Entries marked ``renorm $\uparrow$'' have $x_{\text{prev}} < L$;
the decoder reads one or more bits from the stream to bring them into
range before they become the new working state.  In a full
implementation these post-renorm values are stored in the table
directly, so no computation happens at decode time.

\medskip
\textit{Example interpretation:} Slot $q = 3$ holds symbol $A$ with
$x_{\text{prev}} = 7$.  If the decoder arrives at slot 3, it knows the
symbol was $A$ and the encoder's state just before encoding that $A$
was $7$ (or brought to $7$ after upward renorm).

\medskip
\textbf{3. Decode one step using the table.}

Suppose the encoder's final state is $x = 8$ and we read
$q = x \bmod L = 8 \bmod 8 = 0$.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{sym[0]} $= A$;\quad $x_{\text{prev}}(0) = 4 < L = 8$.
  \item Renormalise upward: read one bit $b$ from the stream;
        $x_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow (4 \ll 1)\mid b$.
        If $b = 1$: $x_{\text{prev}} = 9 \geq 8$, done.
        If $b = 0$: $x_{\text{prev}} = 8 \geq 8$, done.
  \item The decoder recovers symbol $A$ and the next working state
        in a single table lookup plus one bit-read --- no arithmetic.
\end{itemize}

The complete decode walk-through for a full sequence follows exactly
the same pattern as the rANS decoding example, with arithmetic
replaced by table lookups.
\end{examplebox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Summary of Key Concepts}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{center}
\rowcolors{2}{green!8}{white}
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{4.5cm}p{9cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Concept} & \textbf{One-Line Summary} \\
\midrule
State $x$ & A single integer carrying all compressed data so far \\
Normalised range $[L, 2L)$ & Keeps $x$ bounded; renorm emits/reads bits \\
Pre-normalisation & Emit bits \emph{before} the formula so $x \in [f_s, 2f_s)$ \\
Slot invariant & $x \bmod L \in [c_s, c_s+f_s)$ must hold after update \\
rANS encode formula &
  $x \leftarrow \lfloor x/f_s \rfloor \cdot L + c_s + (x \bmod f_s)$,
  applied after pre-norm \\
rANS decode formula &
  $x_{\text{prev}} = f_s \cdot \lfloor x/L \rfloor + (x \bmod L) - c_s$ \\
tANS & Replaces arithmetic with a pre-built lookup table \\
Symbol spread & Distributes $f_s$ slots to symbol $s$ across the table \\
Encode backwards & Encode block right-to-left so decoded order is correct \\
Redundancy & Approaches 0 as table size $L \to \infty$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Consider the alphabet $\{X, Y\}$ with frequencies $f_X = 3$, $f_Y = 1$,

    \item Verify your answer by decoding the result step-by-step,
