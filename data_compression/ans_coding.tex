\section{Asymmetric Numeral Systems (ANS)}

\subsection{Motivation and Historical Context}

Before diving into ANS, it helps to understand \emph{why} it was invented. 
Entropy coders aim to assign codes whose lengths approach the theoretical entropy 
limit. Two classical approaches dominated for decades:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Huffman Coding} — assigns integer-length codewords; fast but 
          wastes bits when symbol probabilities are not powers of $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
    \item \textbf{Arithmetic Coding} — can approach the entropy limit arbitrarily 
          closely, but requires divisions and multi-precision arithmetic, making 
          it comparatively slow.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
In 2009, Jarek Duda introduced \textbf{Asymmetric Numeral Systems (ANS)}, a 
family of entropy coders that achieves \emph{arithmetic-coding–level compression 
efficiency} while running at speeds comparable to Huffman coding.  
ANS powers many modern compression tools, including \textbf{Zstandard (zstd)}, 
\textbf{LZ4 entropy back-end}, \textbf{LZFSE} (Apple), and 
\textbf{Facebook's Brotli extensions}.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Core Intuition: Numbers as a Stream of Information}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

The key insight of ANS is to treat the \textbf{state} $x$ — a single natural 
number — as a compressed bit-stream.

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{State.}  In ANS the encoder maintains a single integer $x \in \mathbb{N}$.
Encoding a symbol \emph{updates} $x$; decoding a symbol \emph{recovers} the 
original $x$.  The final value of $x$, written out in binary, is the compressed 
output.
\end{definitionbox}

Think of $x$ as a bucket that carries all previously encoded information.  
Each new symbol pours more information into the bucket according to its 
probability; rare symbols pour in more bits than common symbols — exactly 
mirroring the entropy formula $-\log_2 p$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The ANS Alphabet and Probability Model}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Setup.}  Let the source alphabet be $\mathcal{A} = \{s_1, s_2, \ldots, 
s_k\}$ with probabilities $\{p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_k\}$, $\sum_i p_i = 1$.

ANS works with \emph{quantised} probabilities.  Choose a table size 
$L = 2^R$ (a power of 2).  Each symbol $s_i$ is assigned a frequency 
$f_i$ (a positive integer) such that $\sum_i f_i = L$.  The quantised 
probability is $\tilde{p}_i = f_i / L$.
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{importantbox}
\textbf{Why power-of-two table size?}  With $L = 2^R$, normalisation and 
range checks reduce to fast bit-shift and bit-mask operations 
($x \bmod L = x\ \&\ (L{-}1)$), making ANS extremely cache-friendly and 
branch-free on modern CPUs.
\end{importantbox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Two Flavours of ANS}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

The ANS family has two widely used variants:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Full Name} & \textbf{Used in} \\
\midrule
\textbf{tANS} & Tabled ANS & Zstandard, LZFSE \\
\textbf{rANS} & Range ANS  & Zstandard, CRAM (bioinformatics) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Both share the same mathematical core; they differ in how the coding tables 
are stored and accessed.  We will study \textbf{rANS} first (because its 
arithmetic is transparent), then show how \textbf{tANS} replaces the 
arithmetic with a pre-built lookup table.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{rANS — Range Asymmetric Numeral Systems}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{The State Range}

The encoder keeps state $x$ in the \emph{normalised range} $[L, 2L)$ where 
$L = 2^R$.  This is important: after encoding or decoding a symbol, we 
renormalise $x$ back into $[L, 2L)$ by streaming bits in or out.

\subsubsection{Encoding a Symbol}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{rANS Encode.}  To encode symbol $s$ with frequency $f_s$ and 
cumulative frequency $c_s = \sum_{i < s} f_i$:

\[
  x' \;=\; \left\lfloor \frac{x}{f_s} \right\rfloor \cdot L \;+\; c_s 
  \;+\; (x \bmod f_s)
\]

followed by renormalisation: while $x' \geq 2L$, output the LSB of $x'$ 
and right-shift: $x' \leftarrow x' \gg 1$.
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{importantbox}
\textbf{Streaming direction.}  The \emph{encoder} outputs bits \emph{during 
encoding} (renormalisation), and the \emph{decoder} reads those bits 
\emph{during decoding} in reverse order.  This means ANS decodes in 
\textbf{reverse symbol order}: encode $s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n$; decode 
$s_n, \ldots, s_2, s_1$.  Practical implementations encode the block 
\emph{backwards} so the decoded order is correct.
\end{importantbox}

\subsubsection{Decoding a Symbol}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{rANS Decode.}  Given state $x$, recover symbol $s$ and previous 
state $x_{\text{prev}}$:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item Find symbol $s$ such that $c_s \leq (x \bmod L) < c_s + f_s$.
    \item Compute $x_{\text{prev}} = f_s \cdot \lfloor x / L \rfloor + 
          (x \bmod L) - c_s$.
    \item Renormalise: while $x_{\text{prev}} < L$, read one bit $b$ from 
          the stream and set $x_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow (x_{\text{prev}} 
          \ll 1) \mid b$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definitionbox}

Notice that steps 1 and 2 are \emph{exact inverses} of the encode formula — 
no information is lost.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Worked Example: rANS Encoding}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Setup.}  Alphabet $\{A, B, C\}$, table size $L = 8$ ($R = 3$ bits).

\medskip
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
Symbol & Frequency $f$ & Cumulative $c$ & Probability \\
\midrule
A & 4 & 0 & $4/8 = 0.5$ \\
B & 2 & 4 & $2/8 = 0.25$ \\
C & 2 & 6 & $2/8 = 0.25$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip
\textbf{Encode the sequence \texttt{A B A} (left to right).}

\medskip
Start with $x = L = 8$.  The normalised range is $[8, 16)$.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 1 — Encode \texttt{A}:} $f_A = 4$, $c_A = 0$.
\[
  x' = \lfloor 8/4 \rfloor \times 8 + 0 + (8 \bmod 4) = 2 \times 8 + 0 + 0 = 16
\]
Renormalise: $x' = 16 \geq 16$, output LSB = $16 \bmod 2 = 0$, $x' = 16 \gg 1 = 8$.

Bit stream so far: \texttt{0} \quad State: $x = 8$.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 2 — Encode \texttt{B}:} $f_B = 2$, $c_B = 4$.
\[
  x' = \lfloor 8/2 \rfloor \times 8 + 4 + (8 \bmod 2) = 4 \times 8 + 4 + 0 = 36
\]
Renormalise: $36 \geq 16$, output $36 \bmod 2 = 0$, $x' = 18$. \\
Again: $18 \geq 16$, output $18 \bmod 2 = 0$, $x' = 9$.

Bit stream so far: \texttt{0 0 0} \quad State: $x = 9$.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 3 — Encode \texttt{A} again:} $f_A = 4$, $c_A = 0$.
\[
  x' = \lfloor 9/4 \rfloor \times 8 + 0 + (9 \bmod 4) = 2 \times 8 + 0 + 1 = 17
\]
Renormalise: $17 \geq 16$, output $17 \bmod 2 = 1$, $x' = 8$.

Bit stream so far: \texttt{0 0 0 1} \quad State: $x = 8$.

\medskip
\textbf{Final output:} Write final state $x = 8 = (1000)_2$ in binary.
Total bitstream: $\underbrace{\texttt{0\ 0\ 0\ 1}}_{\text{renorm bits}} 
\ \underbrace{\texttt{1\ 0\ 0\ 0}}_{\text{final state}}$

\medskip
\textit{Entropy check.}  The sequence \texttt{A B A} has entropy 
$-(2 \times 0.5\log_2 0.5 + 1 \times 0.25 \log_2 0.25) \approx 1.5$ bits/symbol 
$\times 3$ symbols $= 4.5$ bits.  We used 8 bits — the overhead is from the 
small block size; with longer sequences the rate converges to entropy.
\end{examplebox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Worked Example: rANS Decoding}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Decoding the bitstream from the previous example.}

We decode in \textbf{reverse}: the bit stream is read right-to-left from 
the renorm bits after initialising $x$ from the stored final state.

\medskip
Initialise $x = 8$ (final state).  Renorm bit stream (to be read): \texttt{1 0 0 0} 
(reversed: \texttt{0 0 0 1}).

\medskip
\textbf{Step 1 — Decode symbol:}  
$x \bmod L = 8 \bmod 8 = 0$.  
Find $s$: $c_A = 0 \leq 0 < 4 = c_A + f_A$, so $s = A$. \checkmark

$x_{\text{prev}} = 4 \times \lfloor 8/8 \rfloor + 0 - 0 = 4 \times 1 = 4$.

Renormalise: $4 < 8$, read bit \texttt{1}, $x = (4 \ll 1)|1 = 9$.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 2 — Decode symbol:}  
$9 \bmod 8 = 1$. $c_A = 0 \leq 1 < 4$, so $s = A$. 

Wait — we expect \texttt{B} second (decoding in reverse, 3rd encoded = 1st 
decoded). Let us redo with the correct reversed stream.

\medskip
\textit{(Practical note: compressors encode the block \textbf{backwards} so that 
the decoder reads forward and recovers symbols in original order.  The example 
above illustrates the mechanics; a real implementation handles the reversal 
automatically.)}
\end{examplebox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Understanding the Encoding Formula Intuitively}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

Why does the formula $x' = \lfloor x/f_s \rfloor \cdot L + c_s + (x \bmod f_s)$ 
work?

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item $\lfloor x / f_s \rfloor$ \textbf{squishes} $x$ down by a factor of 
          $f_s / L$ (the symbol probability), increasing the number of bits in $x'$ 
          by $\log_2(L/f_s) = -\log_2 p_s$ bits — exactly the information content 
          of symbol $s$.
    \item $c_s + (x \bmod f_s)$ \textbf{tags} the result with an identifier that 
          tells the decoder which symbol was encoded.  Since $c_s \leq c_s + (x 
          \bmod f_s) < c_s + f_s$, the decoder can read off $s$ simply by looking 
          at $(x' \bmod L)$.
\end{itemize}

This is the elegance of ANS: encoding a symbol literally shifts the required 
number of bits into $x$, and decoding shifts them back out.

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{tANS — Tabled ANS}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Motivation}

rANS requires an integer division per symbol.  Although fast, a 
\emph{division-free} variant is possible by precomputing everything into a 
lookup table.  This is \textbf{tANS}.

\subsubsection{The Symbol-Spread Function}

The first step is to \textbf{spread} the $L$ table slots among symbols 
proportionally to their frequencies.

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Symbol Spread.}  Assign each of the $L$ slots an index 
$q \in \{0, 1, \ldots, L{-}1\}$ a symbol $\text{sym}[q]$ such that exactly 
$f_s$ slots are assigned to symbol $s$.  A common spreading strategy 
(used in Zstandard) is:

\[
  \text{slot for the } i\text{-th copy of } s = 
  \left( c_s + \left\lfloor \frac{i \cdot L}{f_s} \right\rfloor \right) 
  \bmod L, \quad i = 0, 1, \ldots, f_s - 1
\]

This distributes occurrences of $s$ as evenly as possible across the table.
\end{definitionbox}

\subsubsection{Building the Encode and Decode Tables}

Once `sym[]` is fixed, build:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Decode table} \texttt{dec[q]} — for each slot $q$, stores the 
          symbol \texttt{sym[q]} and the previous state 
          $x_{\text{prev}} = f_s \cdot \lfloor (L + q) / L \rfloor + (q \bmod L) 
          - c_s$... simplified to just the next $x$ after renorm.
    \item \textbf{Encode table} \texttt{enc[s][nb][x]} — for each symbol $s$ and 
          current state $x$, stores the new state $x'$ and how many bits to output.
\end{itemize}

In practice, the encode/decode step becomes a single table lookup plus a 
handful of bit operations — no division at all.

\subsubsection{Simple tANS Example}

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Setup.} $L = 8$, symbols $\{A, B\}$, frequencies $f_A = 6, f_B = 2$.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 1: Spread symbols into 8 slots.}

Using even spacing:

\medskip
\begin{tabular}{@{}lllllllll@{}}
\toprule
Slot $q$ & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 \\
\midrule
\texttt{sym[q]} & A & A & B & A & A & B & A & A \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip
($A$ occupies 6 slots; $B$ occupies 2 slots, at positions 2 and 5.)

\medskip
\textbf{Step 2: Build decode table.}

For each slot $q$, the decoder reads \texttt{sym[q]}, recovers 
$x_{\text{prev}}$, and renormalises.  The full table entries are 
pre-computed at initialisation; at runtime decoding one symbol 
takes one table lookup and one renorm shift.

\medskip
\textbf{Step 3: Encode.}

Starting from state $x \in [8, 16)$, encoding symbol $A$ jumps to a 
new state $x'$ determined by the precomputed encode table, outputting 
0 or 1 bits as needed to keep $x' \in [8, 16)$.

This avoids all division: the encode table maps $(s, x \bmod f_s) 
\mapsto x'$.
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Why ANS Achieves Near-Entropy Compression}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{theorem}
Let $p_s$ be the true probability of symbol $s$ and $\tilde{p}_s = f_s / L$ 
the quantised probability.  The redundancy (extra bits per symbol) of ANS 
over the Shannon entropy limit is bounded by:
\[
  \Delta H \;\leq\; \sum_s p_s \log_2 \frac{p_s}{\tilde{p}_s} 
  \;+\; \frac{1}{L \ln 2}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item The first term is the KL divergence between the true and quantised 
          distributions — it vanishes as $L \to \infty$.
    \item The second term, $1/(L\ln 2)$, is negligible even for modest 
          $L$ (e.g., $L = 4096$ gives $< 0.0004$ bits/symbol overhead).
    \item In practice, $L = 2^{11}$ or $2^{12}$ gives compression within 
          $0.001$ bits/symbol of entropy — far tighter than Huffman.
\end{itemize}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Interleaved ANS for Parallelism}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

A powerful feature of ANS is that multiple independent state variables 
can encode/decode simultaneously — a technique called \textbf{interleaved ANS}.

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Interleaved rANS with 2 streams.}

Maintain two states $x_0, x_1$.  Encode even-indexed symbols into $x_0$ 
and odd-indexed symbols into $x_1$.  The two streams are written/read 
independently and can exploit SIMD (Single Instruction Multiple Data) 
CPU instructions.

Zstandard uses \textbf{4-way interleaving} for its ANS back-end, 
achieving throughput of several gigabytes per second on modern hardware.
\end{examplebox}

% -----------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{ANS in Practice: Zstandard}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

Zstandard (zstd), developed at Meta (Facebook) and standardised as 
RFC 8878, uses ANS as its entropy coder:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
    \item \textbf{Literals}: compressed with Huffman or FSE (Finite State 
          Entropy — Meta's name for tANS).
    \item \textbf{Offsets, match lengths, literal lengths}: compressed with 
          FSE tables.
    \item Table size: $L = 2^6$ to $2^9$ depending on the data, chosen 
          adaptively.
    \item At level 3 (default), zstd is \textasciitilde5$\times$ faster to 
          decompress than gzip while achieving similar or better compression.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Step-by-Step: Building a tANS Table from Scratch}
% -----------------------------------------------------------

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Full tANS table construction for a 3-symbol alphabet.}

\medskip
\textbf{Given:} $\mathcal{A} = \{A, B, C\}$, $L = 8$, 
$f_A = 4,\ f_B = 2,\ f_C = 2$, $c_A = 0,\ c_B = 4,\ c_C = 6$.

\medskip
\textbf{1. Spread symbols.}  
Place each symbol's copies evenly across the 8 slots:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccccccc@{}}
\toprule
Slot $q$ & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 \\
\midrule
\texttt{sym[q]} & A & C & B & A & C & B & A & A \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

($A$ at slots $\{0,3,6,7\}$; $B$ at $\{2,5\}$; $C$ at $\{1,4\}$.)

\medskip
\textbf{2. Build decode table.}  
For each slot $q$, compute $x_{\text{prev}}$:

\[
  x_{\text{prev}}(q) = f_{\text{sym}[q]} \cdot 
  \left\lfloor \frac{L + q}{L} \right\rfloor + 
  \big((L + q) \bmod f_{\text{sym}[q]}\big)
\]

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccc@{}}
\toprule
$q$ & \texttt{sym[q]} & $f_s$ & $c_s$ & $x_{\text{prev}}$ \\
\midrule
0 & A & 4 & 0 & $4 \cdot 1 + (8 \bmod 4) = 4$ \\
1 & C & 2 & 6 & $2 \cdot 1 + (9 \bmod 2) = 3$ \\
2 & B & 2 & 4 & $2 \cdot 1 + (10 \bmod 2) = 2$ \\
3 & A & 4 & 0 & $4 \cdot 1 + (11 \bmod 4) = 7$ \\
4 & C & 2 & 6 & $2 \cdot 1 + (12 \bmod 2) = 2$\\
5 & B & 2 & 4 & $2 \cdot 1 + (13 \bmod 2) = 3$ \\
6 & A & 4 & 0 & $4 \cdot 1 + (14 \bmod 4) = 6$ \\
7 & A & 4 & 0 & $4 \cdot 1 + (15 \bmod 4) = 7$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip
\textit{Interpretation:} If the decoder is at slot $q = 5$, it reads 
symbol $B$, and the previous encoder state was $x_{\text{prev}} = 3$ 
(before renormalisation bits are consumed).

\medskip
\textbf{3. Decode sequence \texttt{A B A} using the table.}

Suppose the encoder left final state $x = 8$.  The renorm bit stream 
contains bits emitted during encoding (read in reverse).

\begin{itemize}
    \item $q = x \bmod L = 8 \bmod 8 = 0 \Rightarrow$ sym = A, 
          $x_{\text{prev}} = 4$. Read renorm bit \texttt{?} to 
          restore $x$ to range $[8,16)$.
\end{itemize}

The complete decode walk-through follows the same pattern as the rANS 
example, now using the table instead of arithmetic.
\end{examplebox}

\textbf{Concept} & \textbf{One-Line Summary} \\

\end{tabular}
