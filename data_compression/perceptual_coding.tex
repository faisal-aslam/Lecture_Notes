\subsection{Motivation: Why Is Audio Different from Images?}

\subsubsection{The Raw Cost of Digital Audio}

A single CD-quality audio signal requires:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Sampling rate: 44,100 samples per second (slightly above the Nyquist requirement for the 20 Hz--20 kHz audible range)
    \item Bit depth: 16 bits per sample
    \item Channels: 2 (stereo)
\end{itemize}
The raw bit rate is:
\[
\text{Bit rate} = 44{,}100 \text{ samples/s} \times 16 \text{ bits/sample} \times 2 \text{ channels} = \mathbf{1{,}411{,}200 \text{ bits/s}} \approx 1.4\text{ Mbit/s}.
\]
One minute of CD-quality stereo audio occupies approximately 10 MB.

\subsubsection{Why Not Just Use Lossless Compression?}

Lossless audio codecs (FLAC, Apple Lossless) achieve roughly 2:1 compression on typical music, reducing 1.4 Mbit/s to about 700 kbit/s. MP3 achieves 10:1 to 14:1 compression (128--320 kbit/s) \emph{while being perceptually transparent for many listeners under typical conditions}.

\begin{importantbox}
\textbf{The Central Insight of Perceptual Coding:}
The human auditory system does not perceive all information in a sound signal equally. Large amounts of the signal can be discarded entirely without the listener being able to detect the loss. The goal of MP3 and AAC is not to minimise mathematical error (as in lossless coding) but to minimise \emph{perceptible} error.
\end{importantbox}

\subsubsection{The Contrast with JPEG}

In the JPEG chapter we saw that the human visual system is less sensitive to high-frequency spatial detail. The psychoacoustic model in MP3 is the \emph{audio counterpart}: it determines, dynamically and per-frequency-band, how much quantisation noise the ear will tolerate at each instant in time. Both systems share the same three-stage skeleton:

\subsection{The Human Auditory System: Three Pillars of Perception}

\subsubsection{Pillar 1 --- The Absolute Threshold of Hearing}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Absolute Threshold of Hearing (ATH):} The minimum sound pressure level (SPL) at each frequency that an average human can detect in a perfectly quiet environment. Any sound \emph{below} the ATH at a given frequency is completely inaudible and can be discarded at effectively zero perceptual cost.
\end{definitionbox}

The ATH is highly non-uniform across the audible spectrum. A useful approximation (Terhardt's formula) in dB SPL is:
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{ATH}}(f) = 3.64\left(\frac{f}{1000}\right)^{-0.8} - 6.5\,e^{-0.6(f/1000 - 3.3)^2} + 10^{-3}\!\left(\frac{f}{1000}\right)^4,
\label{eq:ath}
\end{equation}
where $f$ is in Hz and the result is in dB SPL.

\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Example 1: Applying the ATH formula step by step.}

\medskip
Evaluate $T_{\text{ATH}}$ at four frequencies using Eq.~\eqref{eq:ath} and decide which bands can be discarded for free.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Band & $f$ (Hz) & Signal (dB SPL) & $T_{\text{ATH}}$ (dB SPL) & Decision \\
\midrule
A &   200 & 18 & ? & ? \\
B &  1000 & 25 & ? & ? \\
C &  8000 & 10 & ? & ? \\
D & 16000 &  5 & ? & ? \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Let $u = f/1000$ for brevity. We compute the three terms $T_1$, $T_2$, $T_3$ separately.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Band A: $f = 200$ Hz, $u = 0.2$.}
\begin{align*}
T_1 &= 3.64 \times (0.2)^{-0.8} = 3.64 \times 6.899 = 25.11\ \text{dB}, \\
T_2 &= 6.5\,e^{-0.6(0.2 - 3.3)^2} = 6.5\,e^{-5.766} \approx 0.020\ \text{dB}, \\
T_3 &= 10^{-3}(0.2)^4 \approx 0\ \text{dB}, \\
T_{\text{ATH}}(200) &\approx 25.1\ \text{dB SPL}.
\end{align*}
Signal (18 dB) $<$ ATH (25.1 dB): \textbf{inaudible -- 0 bits needed.}

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Band B: $f = 1000$ Hz, $u = 1.0$.}
\begin{align*}
T_1 &= 3.64 \times 1^{-0.8} = 3.64\ \text{dB}, \\
T_2 &= 6.5\,e^{-0.6(1.0-3.3)^2} = 6.5 \times 0.0418 = 0.27\ \text{dB}, \\
T_3 &= 10^{-3} \times 1 = 0.001\ \text{dB}, \\
T_{\text{ATH}}(1000) &\approx 3.37\ \text{dB SPL}.
\end{align*}
Signal (25 dB) $>$ ATH (3.4 dB): \textbf{audible -- allocate bits.} SMR $= 25 - 3.4 = 21.6$ dB, so $\lceil 21.6/6 \rceil = 4$ bits minimum.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Band C: $f = 8000$ Hz, $u = 8.0$.}
\begin{align*}
T_1 &= 3.64 \times (8)^{-0.8} = 3.64 \times 0.1768 = 0.643\ \text{dB}, \\
T_2 &\approx 0\ \text{dB} \quad (e^{-13.25} \approx 0), \\
T_3 &= 10^{-3}(8)^4 = 4.096\ \text{dB}, \\
T_{\text{ATH}}(8000) &\approx 4.74\ \text{dB SPL}.
\end{align*}
Signal (10 dB) $>$ ATH (4.7 dB): \textbf{audible.} SMR $= 10 - 4.7 = 5.3$ dB, so $\lceil 5.3/6 \rceil = 1$ bit.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Band D: $f = 16000$ Hz, $u = 16.0$.}
\begin{align*}
T_1 &= 3.64 \times (16)^{-0.8} = 0.347\ \text{dB}, \\
T_2 &\approx 0\ \text{dB}, \\
T_3 &= 10^{-3}(16)^4 = 65.5\ \text{dB}, \\
T_{\text{ATH}}(16000) &\approx 65.8\ \text{dB SPL}.
\end{align*}
Signal (5 dB) $\ll$ ATH (65.8 dB): \textbf{inaudible by 60.8 dB -- 0 bits needed.}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Summary:} Before any masking is even considered, the ATH eliminates Bands A and D entirely. Band B requires $\geq 4$ bits; Band C requires only 1 bit. This demonstrates the ATH alone contributing free bit savings.
\end{examplebox}

\subsubsection{Pillar 2 --- Simultaneous Masking (Frequency Masking)}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Simultaneous Masking:} When a loud \emph{masker} tone is present, it raises the hearing threshold in nearby frequency bands, making softer \emph{maskee} sounds inaudible even though they are above the ATH. Masking spreads asymmetrically: more strongly \emph{above} the masker frequency (upward spread) than below it.
\end{definitionbox}

\paragraph{Tonal vs.\ Noise Maskers.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Tonal maskers:} Narrow spectral peaks (e.g., a pure tone). Strong, localised masking with a sharp spreading function.
    \item \textbf{Noise maskers:} Broad spectral humps (e.g., a drum hit). Weaker, broader masking.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Setup.} Two masker tones are present simultaneously:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Masker 1:} 500 Hz at 70 dB SPL (tonal).
    \item \textbf{Masker 2:} 4000 Hz at 65 dB SPL (tonal).

\noindent\textbf{Spreading functions (simplified didactic model):}

\Delta S_{\text{up}} = 10 + 2.5\,\Delta z \quad (\Delta z > 0),

\noindent\textbf{Step 1 --- Convert all frequencies to Bark.}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule

\midrule

6000 Hz  & 19.5 & Maskee R \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2 --- Compute masked thresholds. The effective mask is $\max(\text{ATH}, T_{m1}, T_{m2})$.}

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Maskee P (1000 Hz, 8.5 Bark):}

From Masker 1 (500 Hz, 4.9 Bark, 70 dB, upward $\Delta z = +3.6$):
\[
\Delta S = 10 + 2.5\times3.6 = 19.0\ \text{dB}, \qquad T_{m1}(P) = 70 - 19.0 = 51.0\ \text{dB}.
\]
From Masker 2 (4000 Hz, 17.5 Bark, 65 dB, downward $|\Delta z| = 9.0$):
\[
\Delta S = 25 + 10\times9.0 = 115\ \text{dB}, \qquad T_{m2}(P) = \max(0,\ 65 - 115) = 0\ \text{dB}.
\]
Effective: $T_{\text{mask}}(P) = \max(3.4,\ 51.0,\ 0) = \mathbf{51.0\ \text{dB SPL}}$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Maskee Q (2500 Hz, 15.0 Bark):}

From Masker 1 (upward $\Delta z = +10.1$):
\[
\Delta S = 10 + 2.5\times10.1 = 35.25\ \text{dB}, \qquad T_{m1}(Q) = 70 - 35.25 = 34.75\ \text{dB}.
\]
From Masker 2 (downward $|\Delta z| = 2.5$):
\[
\Delta S = 25 + 10\times2.5 = 50\ \text{dB}, \qquad T_{m2}(Q) = \max(0,\ 65 - 50) = 15\ \text{dB}.
\]
Effective: $T_{\text{mask}}(Q) = \max(0,\ 34.75,\ 15) = \mathbf{34.75\ \text{dB SPL}}$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Maskee R (6000 Hz, 19.5 Bark):}

From Masker 1 (upward $\Delta z = +14.6$):
\[
\Delta S = 10 + 2.5\times14.6 = 46.5\ \text{dB}, \qquad T_{m1}(R) = 70 - 46.5 = 23.5\ \text{dB}.
\]
From Masker 2 (upward $\Delta z = +2.0$):
\[
\Delta S = 10 + 2.5\times2.0 = 15.0\ \text{dB}, \qquad T_{m2}(R) = 65 - 15.0 = 50.0\ \text{dB}.
\]
Effective: $T_{\text{mask}}(R) = \max(5.5,\ 23.5,\ 50.0) = \mathbf{50.0\ \text{dB SPL}}$.

\subsubsection{The Bark Scale and Critical Bands}
\label{sec:bark}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Critical Band:} The range of frequencies that activate the same region of the basilar membrane. There are approximately 25 critical bands spanning 20 Hz--20 kHz.

\textbf{Bark Frequency:} A frequency scale calibrated to critical-band width:
\begin{equation}
z(f) = 13\arctan\!\left(0.00076\,f\right) + 3.5\arctan\!\left(\frac{f}{7500}\right)^2,
\label{eq:bark}
\end{equation}
where $f$ is in Hz and $z$ is in Bark.
\end{definitionbox}

\subsubsection{Pillar 3 --- Temporal Masking}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Temporal Masking:} The masking effect extends \emph{in time} around a loud sound event.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Post-masking (forward masking):} A loud sound continues to mask quieter sounds for up to 100--200 ms \emph{after} it ends.
  \item \textbf{Pre-masking (backward masking):} A loud sound masks quieter sounds up to 5--20 ms \emph{before} it begins, because the brain integrates sound over a short window before making a perceptual decision.
\end{itemize}
\end{definitionbox}

\begin{importantbox}
\textbf{The Three Pillars Working Together:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{ATH} sets a global noise floor below which everything is free.
  \item \textbf{Simultaneous masking} dynamically raises the threshold based on what loud signals are present right now, often by 20--60 dB.
  \item \textbf{Temporal masking} allows coarser coding in frames just before and after loud transients.
\end{enumerate}
Together these effects mean the perceptual noise floor is often 40--60 dB above the ATH --- this is where the 10:1 compression ratio comes from.
\end{importantbox}

\subsection{The Modified Discrete Cosine Transform (MDCT)}

\subsubsection{Why Not the DCT?}

The plain DCT processes blocks independently, creating discontinuities at block boundaries that manifest as a ``flanging'' or ``warbling'' sound. A second problem is the trade-off between frequency resolution and time resolution.

\subsubsection{The MDCT: Overlapping Blocks with Perfect Reconstruction}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Modified Discrete Cosine Transform (MDCT):} An overlapping transform where each block of $2N$ input samples produces $N$ output coefficients. Adjacent blocks overlap by 50\%. Despite the overlap, the MDCT satisfies \textbf{perfect reconstruction}: the overlapping IMDCT blocks, when added using overlap-add, exactly recover the original signal (before quantisation).
\end{definitionbox}

\paragraph{MDCT formula.}
\begin{equation}
X(k) = \sum_{n=0}^{2N-1} x(n)\,w(n)\, \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{N}\!\left(n+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{N}{2}\right) \left(k+\frac{1}{2}\right)\right), \quad k=0,\ldots,N-1,
\label{eq:mdct}
\end{equation}
where $w(n)$ is the analysis window.

\paragraph{Key properties.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{50\% overlap:} Each frame of $N$ coefficients describes $2N$ input samples.
  \item \textbf{Critical sampling:} Output coefficients ($N$) equals new input samples per frame --- no data expansion.
  \item \textbf{TDAC property} (Time-Domain Aliasing Cancellation): Aliasing introduced by the overlap exactly cancels during overlap-add, giving perfect reconstruction.
\end{enumerate}

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Example 3: MDCT computation on a minimal block ($N=2$).}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Setup.} $N = 2$, block size $= 4$ samples, rectangular window $w(n) = 1$.
\[
\mathbf{x} = [x(0),\ x(1),\ x(2),\ x(3)] = [1,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4].
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The MDCT formula for $N=2$:}
\[
X(k) = \sum_{n=0}^{3} x(n)\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\left(n + \frac{3}{2}\right)\left(k + \frac{1}{2}\right)\right).
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Computing $X(0)$} ($k = 0$, argument $= \frac{\pi}{2}(n+\frac{3}{2})\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \frac{\pi(n+1.5)}{4}$):
\begin{align*}
n=0: &\quad 1 \times \cos(67.5^\circ) = 1 \times 0.3827 = 0.3827, \\
n=1: &\quad 2 \times \cos(112.5^\circ) = 2 \times (-0.9239) = -1.8478, \\
n=2: &\quad 3 \times \cos(157.5^\circ) = 3 \times (-0.9239) = -2.7717, \\
n=3: &\quad 4 \times \cos(202.5^\circ) = 4 \times (-0.3827) = -1.5307.
\end{align*}
\[
X(0) = 0.3827 - 1.8478 - 2.7717 - 1.5307 = \mathbf{-5.7675}.
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Computing $X(1)$} ($k = 1$, argument $= \frac{3\pi(n+1.5)}{4}$):
\begin{align*}
n=0: &\quad 1 \times \cos(202.5^\circ) = -0.3827, \\
n=1: &\quad 2 \times \cos(337.5^\circ) = 2 \times 0.9239 = +1.8478, \\
n=2: &\quad 3 \times \cos(112.5^\circ) = 3 \times (-0.3827) = -1.1481, \\
n=3: &\quad 4 \times \cos(247.5^\circ) = 4 \times (-0.3827) = -1.5307.
\end{align*}
\[
X(1) = -0.3827 + 1.8478 - 1.1481 - 1.5307 = \mathbf{-1.2137}.
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Interpretation.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item 4 input samples $\to$ 2 MDCT coefficients: \emph{critical sampling confirmed.}
  \item $|X(0)| \gg |X(1)|$: the rising ramp $[1,2,3,4]$ has strong low-frequency energy --- correctly captured in the lowest-frequency MDCT coefficient.
  \item In real MP3 with $N = 576$: $2N = 1152$ input samples produce 576 MDCT frequency lines per granule.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{What if we used a pure zero input $\mathbf{x} = [0,0,0,0]$?}
Then $X(0) = X(1) = 0$ trivially --- an MDCT of silence produces all-zero coefficients, which Huffman coding collapses to a handful of bits. This is why silence is compressed so aggressively in MP3.
\end{examplebox}

\subsubsection{Window Switching: Handling Transients}

For stationary signals, MP3 uses \textbf{long blocks} ($N=576$). When a transient is detected, it switches to \textbf{short blocks} ($N=192$) to avoid pre-echo.

\begin{warningbox}
\textbf{Pre-Echo: The Most Audible MP3 Artefact at Low Bit-Rates.}

A long block is analysed as a whole. If a loud transient appears mid-block, coarse quantisation spreads noise across the entire block after IMDCT --- including the silence \emph{before} the transient. You hear a faint ``pre-echo'' before the event occurs.

\textbf{Solution:} Switch to short blocks (8.7 ms each). Noise cannot spread more than 8.7 ms, and post-masking from the loud transient hides any noise in the preceding short block.
\end{warningbox}

\subsubsection{Stage 1 --- Polyphase Quadrature Mirror Filterbank (PQMF)}

The MP3 encoder divides audio into 32 equal-width subbands. Each subband produces 18 samples per frame, giving $32\times18=576$ values transformed by the MDCT.

\subsubsection{Stage 2 --- Psychoacoustic Analysis (in Parallel)}

A separate 1024-point FFT analyses the PCM samples. The psychoacoustic model identifies tonal and noise maskers, computes spreading functions, sums contributions, combines with the ATH, and computes SMR per subband.

\subsubsection{Stage 3 --- Bit Allocation}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Noise Shaping Objective:} Allocate bits such that the quantisation noise in each band is just below the masking threshold --- no more (wastes bits), no fewer (makes noise audible).
\end{definitionbox}

\subsubsection{Stage 4 --- Quantisation and Huffman Coding}

The MDCT coefficients are quantised using a non-uniform scalar quantiser:
\begin{equation}
\hat{X}(k) = \operatorname{nint}\!\left( \frac{|X(k)|^{3/4}}{\text{quantstep}} \right) \times \operatorname{sgn}(X(k)),
\label{eq:mp3quant}
\end{equation}
where the $3/4$ power law approximates perceptual loudness scaling. The quantised values are Huffman coded; zero runs are run-length encoded, analogous to JPEG's End-of-Block (EOB) mechanism.

\subsection{The 6 dB/Bit Rule and Rate--Distortion in Audio}

For a uniform quantiser with step size $\Delta$ and $b$ bits:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SNR_{dB}} \approx 1.76 + 6.02b \quad [\text{dB}].
\label{eq:snrbit}
\end{equation}
\textbf{6 dB per bit rule:} each additional bit improves SNR by approximately 6 dB.

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Example 5: Applying the 6 dB/bit rule --- bits, step size, and verification.}

\medskip
A subband has MDCT coefficients with peak amplitude $A = 1.0$. The required SNR is \textbf{54 dB}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part (a) --- Minimum bits.}
\[
54 \leq 1.76 + 6.02b \implies b \geq \frac{54 - 1.76}{6.02} = 8.68 \implies \boxed{b = 9\ \text{bits}}.
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part (b) --- Quantiser step size.}
\[
\Delta = \frac{2A}{2^b} = \frac{2.0}{512} = \mathbf{0.00391}.
\]
Every MDCT coefficient is rounded to the nearest multiple of 0.00391.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part (c) --- Achieved SNR.}
\[
\mathrm{SNR} = 1.76 + 6.02\times9 = 1.76 + 54.18 = \mathbf{55.94\ \text{dB}}.
\]
Exceeds required 54 dB by 1.94 dB. \checkmark

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part (d) --- Why 8 bits is insufficient.}
\[
\mathrm{SNR}|_{b=8} = 1.76 + 6.02\times8 = 49.92\ \text{dB} < 54\ \text{dB}.
\]
The 49.92 dB SNR falls \emph{above} the masking threshold (we need 54 dB SNR to push noise \emph{below} the threshold). The ninth bit is mandatory.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Context:} Without psychoacoustics, CD quality demands $b = 16$ bits. The model reduces this band to 9 bits --- a $16/9 \approx 1.78\times$ reduction in bit usage for this band alone.
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Pre-Echo and Window Switching: A Detailed Example}

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Example 7: Pre-echo --- quantitative analysis and short-block solution.}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Scenario.} A drum hit occurs at sample 900 within a 1152-sample long block (at 44.1 kHz: 26.1 ms total). Samples 0--899 are silence at $-55$ dB; samples 900--1151 contain the percussive attack at 90 dB SPL.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1 --- Masking analysis with a long block.}

The 1152-sample FFT is dominated by the loud attack. The model computes a masking threshold of $\approx$60 dB for the entire block.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2 --- Bit allocation (long block).}
\[
b = \left\lceil \frac{60}{6.02} \right\rceil = 10\ \text{bits for all bands}.
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 3 --- Quantiser step size and noise level.}
\[
\Delta = \frac{2}{2^{10}} = 0.00195, \qquad
\sigma_q^2 = \frac{\Delta^2}{12} = 3.17\times10^{-7}.
\]
\[
\mathrm{SNR} = 1.76 + 6.02\times10 = 61.96\ \text{dB}.
\]
With signal at 90 dB SPL, noise floor $\approx 90 - 61.96 = 28.0$ dB SPL.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 4 --- The pre-echo problem.}

In the silent region (samples 0--899), the signal is at $-55$ dB. The ATH at 1 kHz is $\approx 3.4$ dB SPL.

\[
\text{Noise level (28 dB)} \gg \text{ATH (3.4 dB)}
\]

The pre-echo is audible by $28 - 3.4 = \mathbf{24.6\ \text{dB}}$ above the threshold. This is a severe, clearly perceptible artefact.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 5 --- Solution: switch to three short blocks (384 samples $= 8.7$ ms each).}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccl}
\toprule
Block & Samples & Time span & Content & Strategy \\
\midrule
A & 0--383   & 0--8.7 ms   & silence            & Use many bits (low masking) \\
B & 384--767 & 8.7--17.4 ms& fading silence     & Moderate allocation \\
C & 768--1151& 17.4--26.1 ms& full attack       & High masking; tolerate noise \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Post-masking from Block C's loud attack lasts up to 200 ms. Block B ends $26.1 - 17.4 = 8.7$ ms before Block C starts. Block A ends 17.4 ms before Block C starts. Both A and B fall within the pre-masking window ($\leq$20 ms before onset), so their noise is temporally masked by the drum.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Result:} Noise in Block A is now contained within 8.7 ms and hidden by the drum's pre-masking. Pre-echo is eliminated.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Trade-off summary:}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Property & Long block & Three short blocks \\
\midrule
Time resolution & 26.1 ms & 8.7 ms \\
Frequency resolution & 576 lines & 192 lines \\
Pre-echo risk & High & Eliminated \\
Psychoacoustic model accuracy & High & Reduced \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\end{examplebox}

\subsubsection{The Pure MDCT Filterbank}

AAC replaces MP3's hybrid filterbank with a single MDCT, giving 1024 frequency lines ($\approx 1.78\times$ better frequency resolution) and eliminating PQMF aliasing errors.

\subsubsection{Temporal Noise Shaping (TNS)}

\begin{definitionbox}
\textbf{Temporal Noise Shaping (TNS):} Applies a prediction filter to MDCT coefficients in the frequency domain, shaping quantisation noise in the time domain --- concentrating it where masking is strongest. This directly addresses pre-echo without requiring window switching.
\end{definitionbox}

\subsubsection{Joint Stereo Coding}

\[
M = \frac{L+R}{2} \quad \text{(mid channel)}, \qquad S = \frac{L-R}{2} \quad \text{(side channel)}.
\]

%----------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{examplebox}
\textbf{Example 8: Mid/Side stereo --- full energy and SNR analysis.}

\medskip
\noindent Two stereo channels:
\[
L(t) = 0.9\sin(\omega t), \qquad R(t) = 0.6\sin(\omega t).
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1 --- Compute M and S.}
\[
M = \frac{0.9+0.6}{2} = 0.75, \qquad S = \frac{0.9-0.6}{2} = 0.15.
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2 --- Power ratio.}
\[
P_M \propto 0.75^2 = 0.5625, \quad P_S \propto 0.15^2 = 0.0225. \quad
\frac{P_M}{P_S} = 25 \quad (14\ \text{dB advantage for M}).
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 3 --- Bit allocation (total budget: 12 bits).}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Strategy & Bits to L or M & Bits to R or S \\
\midrule
Independent L/R & 6 each & 6 each \\
M/S stereo & 9 to M & 3 to S \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

M is allocated more bits because it carries 25$\times$ more power.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 4 --- Quantiser step sizes.}
\[
\Delta_M = \frac{2\times0.75}{2^9} = \frac{1.5}{512} = 0.00293, \qquad
\Delta_S = \frac{2\times0.15}{2^3} = \frac{0.30}{8} = 0.0375.
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 5 --- Reconstruction errors.}
\[
\Delta L = \Delta M + \Delta S = 0.00293 + 0.0375 = 0.0404,
\]
\[
\Delta R = \Delta M - \Delta S = 0.00293 - 0.0375 = -0.0346.
\]

\textbf{SNR for L channel} (M/S):
\[
\mathrm{SNR}_L = 20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{0.9}{0.0404}\right) = 20\log_{10}(22.3) = 26.9\ \text{dB}.
\]

\textbf{SNR for L channel} (independent, 6 bits):
\[
\mathrm{SNR}_L = 1.76 + 6.02\times6 = 37.9\ \text{dB}.
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Discussion.} Here independent coding gives higher SNR because the S channel, despite carrying little energy, is quantised very coarsely (3 bits, step 0.0375), which is large relative to its amplitude of 0.15. M/S stereo is most beneficial when $L \approx R$ (i.e., near-mono content, typical in popular music), where $S \approx 0$ and the 3-bit S quantiser has negligible impact.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Key formula:} $\Delta L = \Delta M + \Delta S$, \ $\Delta R = \Delta M - \Delta S$. When $|\Delta S| \ll |\Delta M|$, both channels are dominated by the high-quality M error. M/S stereo saves $\approx$10--25\% of the total bit budget for typical music.
\end{examplebox}

\noindent\textbf{Stage 4 --- Quantisation.}
\[
\mathrm{SNR}(12\ \text{bits}) = 1.76 + 6.02\times12 = 74.0\ \text{dB} \quad (> 71\ \text{required}).\ \checkmark
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Stage 5 --- Huffman coding.}

Non-zero coefficients: $\approx 18$ (subband 0). Zeros: $576 - 18 = 558$.
Run-length encoding collapses 558 trailing zeros to an End-of-Block token ($\approx$8 bits).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Compression estimate:}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lc}
\toprule
Item & Bits \\
\midrule
Raw PCM (1152 samples $\times$ 16 bits) & 18,432 \\
500 Hz MDCT coefficients ($18 \times 12$) & 216 \\
Huffman overhead + side info & $\approx$ 150 \\
\textbf{Total encoded} & $\approx$ \textbf{366 bits} \\
\textbf{Compression ratio} & $18{,}432/366 \approx \mathbf{50:1}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textit{Note:} This is an idealised two-tone signal. Real music at 128 kbit/s achieves $\approx$11:1.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Decoder.} The 1.5 kHz and 3 kHz tones are absent from the output. The listener cannot detect this: the 75 dB / 500 Hz masker raises the threshold at those frequencies to 49 dB and 35.5 dB respectively --- far above the original 40 dB and 20 dB levels.
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Audio Coding Artefacts}

\subsubsection{Granular Noise (Birdies)}
\textbf{Cause:} Coarse quantisation of MDCT coefficients causes them to oscillate between a handful of levels; IMDCT produces tonal noise not in the original.\\
\textbf{Character:} Faint metallic ``birdlike'' chirping; most audible during quiet passages. Typical threshold: below 80 kbit/s (MP3), 48 kbit/s (AAC).

\subsubsection{Smearing and Loss of Stereo Imaging}
\textbf{Cause:} Aggressive M/S stereo coding at low bit-rates collapses the stereo image toward mono.

\subsubsection{Watery / Phasiness}
\textbf{Cause:} At very low bit-rates, overlap-add reconstruction creates unnatural phase relationships. Sounds ``underwater.''

\subsubsection{Pre-Echo}
\textbf{Cause:} Inadequate bit allocation before a transient spreads quantisation noise into preceding silence.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{MP3 and AAC artefacts: cause and typical bit-rate at which they become audible.}
\label{tab:artefacts}
\begin{tabular}{lccp{5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Artefact} & \textbf{MP3 threshold} & \textbf{AAC threshold} & \textbf{Technical cause} \\
\midrule
Granular noise   & $<80$ kbit/s & $<48$ kbit/s  & Coarse quantisation of MDCT coefficients \\
Pre-echo         & $<96$ kbit/s & $<64$ kbit/s  & Noise spreading into pre-transient silence \\
Stereo collapse  & $<96$ kbit/s & $<64$ kbit/s  & Aggressive M/S / intensity stereo \\
Watery sound     & $<48$ kbit/s & $<32$ kbit/s  & Insufficient MDCT frequency resolution \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Connecting to JPEG: A Unified View}

\begin{connectionbox}
\textbf{The Universal Lossy Compression Paradigm}

\begin{center}
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{5pt}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{JPEG (images)} & \textbf{MP3/AAC (audio)} \\
\midrule
Transform &
  8$\times$8 block DCT &
  MDCT (long: 576/1024, short: 192/128) \\
Perceptual model &
  Fixed Q-table (HVS frequency sensitivity) &
  Dynamic psychoacoustic model (per frame) \\
Quantisation &
  Per-coefficient step size from Q-table &
  Per-subband step size from bit allocator \\
Entropy coding &
  Huffman + RLE + DPCM for DC &
  Huffman (32 tables) + RLE for zero runs \\
Quality control &
  Single quality factor (scales Q-table) &
  Target bit-rate (via psychoacoustic model) \\
\midrule
Blocking artefact &
  8$\times$8 grid at block boundaries &
  Granular noise (spectral birdies) \\
Ringing artefact &
  Gibbs phenomenon at sharp edges &
  Pre-echo at transients \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Key conceptual difference:} JPEG's Q-table is \emph{static}. MP3's psychoacoustic model is \emph{dynamic}: it recomputes masking thresholds $\approx$38 times per second, adapting to the signal in real time.
\end{connectionbox}

\subsection{Summary}

\begin{importantbox}
\textbf{Six Key Insights of Perceptual Audio Coding:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Perception, not mathematics:} The goal is to minimise \emph{audible} distortion, not mathematical MSE.
  \item \textbf{Three masking phenomena:} ATH, simultaneous masking, and temporal masking together define a perceptual noise floor typically 40--60 dB above the ATH.
  \item \textbf{The Bark scale:} Masking operates in critical-band units (Bark), not in Hz. Frequency resolution of the ear is approximately logarithmic.
  \item \textbf{MDCT with overlap:} Reduces blocking artefacts through overlap-add while maintaining critical sampling.
  \item \textbf{Window switching:} Long blocks for stationary signals; short blocks for transients.
  \item \textbf{The same paradigm:} Transform $\to$ perceptual quantisation $\to$ entropy coding is universal. JPEG, MP3, AAC, H.264, H.265, and AV1 all follow it.
\end{enumerate}
\end{importantbox}

\subsection{Exercises}

\begin{exercisebox}
\textbf{(ATH formula application)}
Using Eq.~\eqref{eq:ath}, compute the ATH at $f = 500$ Hz and $f = 4000$ Hz. For each frequency, a tone is present at 15 dB SPL. Determine whether each tone is audible. For audible tones, compute the minimum bits required (use the 6 dB/bit rule with SMR $=$ signal level $-$ ATH).
\end{exercisebox}

\begin{exercisebox}
\textbf{(Bark conversion and single-masker SMR)}
A 800 Hz tonal masker is present at 78 dB SPL. A candidate maskee is at 2000 Hz with a level of 45 dB SPL.
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item Convert 800 Hz and 2000 Hz to Bark using Eq.~\eqref{eq:bark}.
  \item Compute the upward spreading attenuation $\Delta S = 10 + 2.5\,\Delta z$ in dB.
  \item Compute the masked threshold at 2000 Hz.
  \item Compute the SMR. Is the 2000 Hz tone audible?
  \item If audible, how many bits does the encoder allocate? (6 dB/bit rule.)
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercisebox}

\begin{exercisebox}
\textbf{(Two-masker scenario)}
Two tonal maskers: Masker A at 1000 Hz, 80 dB SPL; Masker B at 3000 Hz, 70 dB SPL. A maskee at 2000 Hz, 50 dB SPL. ATH at 2000 Hz $\approx -3$ dB (use 0 dB as floor).

Downward spread: $\Delta S_{\text{down}} = 25 + 10\,|\Delta z|$ dB.
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item Convert 1000, 2000, and 3000 Hz to Bark.
  \item Compute $T_{mA}$ (upward spread from Masker A) and $T_{mB}$ (downward spread from Masker B).
  \item Compute $T_{\text{mask}} = \max(\text{ATH},\ T_{mA},\ T_{mB})$.
  \item Is the maskee audible? Compute the SMR. How many bits are allocated?
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercisebox}

\begin{exercisebox}
\textbf{(Bit allocation with a constrained budget)}
Budget: \textbf{40 bits} for one frame across six subbands.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lc}
\toprule
Band & SMR (dB) \\ \midrule
1 & $+48$ \\ 2 & $+36$ \\ 3 & $-10$ (masked) \\ 4 & $+24$ \\ 5 & $+12$ \\ 6 & $+6$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item Compute minimum bits per band. Total required?
  \item Distribute any spare bits to the highest-SMR bands.
  \item Verify each audible band achieves at least the required SNR.
  \item If budget drops to 20 bits, which band(s) would you sacrifice first and why? Specify the resulting allocation.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercisebox}

\begin{exercisebox}
\textbf{(MDCT computation)}
An MDCT with $N = 2$ (block size 4) uses a rectangular window. Input block: $\mathbf{x} = [3,\ 1,\ -1,\ -3]$ (a falling ramp).
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item Compute $X(0)$ and $X(1)$ using Eq.~\eqref{eq:mdct}.
  \item Compare $|X(0)|$ and $|X(1)|$. Which frequency dominates?
  \item If the amplitude of $X(0)$ were doubled (by scaling the input), by how many dB would the SNR need to increase to maintain the same perceptual quality? (Hint: use the 6 dB/bit rule in reverse.)
  \item Verify that critical sampling holds: how many new samples per frame, and how many output coefficients?
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercisebox}

\begin{exercisebox}
\textbf{(Pre-echo quantitative analysis)}
A long block (1152 samples, 44.1 kHz) contains 800 samples of silence at $-60$ dB, followed by 352 samples of a 2 kHz tone at 85 dB SPL. The bit allocator assigns 8 bits to all subbands.
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item Compute the SNR achieved by an 8-bit quantiser.
  \item With the signal at 85 dB SPL and SNR $= 8$-bit SNR, what is the absolute noise level in dB SPL? Is it above the ATH at 2 kHz ($\approx -3$ dB; use 0 dB)?
  \item By how many dB does the noise in the silent portion exceed the ATH? Is this a severe artefact?
  \item The encoder switches to 3 short blocks (each 384 samples $= 8.7$ ms). Post-masking from the 85 dB attack lasts 200 ms, and pre-masking covers 20 ms before the onset (sample 800, i.e., at $t = 18.1$ ms). Which short block(s) fall within the pre-masking window of the attack, and therefore tolerate coarser quantisation?
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercisebox}

\begin{exercisebox}
\textbf{(Mid/Side stereo analysis)}
Stereo channels: $L(t) = 1.0\sin(\omega t)$, $R(t) = 0.4\sin(\omega t)$.
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item Compute $M$ and $S$ amplitudes.
  \item Compute power ratio $P_M / P_S$ and express in dB.
  \item Total budget: 12 bits. Propose an independent L/R allocation and an M/S allocation. Justify the M/S split.
  \item Compute $\Delta_M$ and $\Delta_S$ for your M/S allocation (assume $A_M$ and $A_S$ from part (a)).
  \item Using $\Delta L = \Delta_M + \Delta_S$, compute the reconstruction error in L. Compute the resulting SNR for the L channel.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercisebox}

\begin{exercisebox}
\textbf{(Design question: perceptual coding for images vs.\ audio)}
JPEG uses a static quantisation table; MP3 uses a dynamic psychoacoustic model recomputed per frame.
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item Why is a static table sufficient for images but not for audio? Consider how content varies across image blocks vs.\ audio frames, and how rapidly masking properties change.
  \item Could a ``dynamic JPEG'' be designed that recomputes the Q-table per 8$\times$8 block based on local visual masking? What information would be needed? How would the decoder know which Q-table was used?
  \item If visual masking similarly raised the effective noise floor by 40 dB in images, how many bits per pixel could be saved using the 6 dB/bit rule? Assuming standard JPEG uses 8 bits per pixel channel, what would the approximate compression ratio become?
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercisebox}
